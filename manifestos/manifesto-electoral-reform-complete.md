## The Problem

The UK's democratic system systematically produces governments that 
cannot deliver evidence-based policy. This is a structural finding, 
not a partisan one.

**The 2024 election was the most disproportional in British history.** 
57.8% of voters were unrepresented — they voted for a candidate who 
didn't win. Labour won a massive majority (411 seats) on just 33.7% 
of the vote. Reform UK won 14.3% of votes but just 0.8% of seats (5). 
The Greens won 6.7% of votes but 0.6% of seats (4). Under PR, the ERS 
projects Labour would have won ~228 seats, Conservatives ~139, Reform 
~100, Greens ~71. The current system doesn't translate votes into 
representation.

**FPTP produces governments incentivised to lie.** The "conspiracy of 
silence" identified by the IFS (→ Taxation) is a direct consequence of 
electoral structure. Under FPTP, elections are decided by a handful of 
swing seats. Parties compete by promising what those marginal voters 
want to hear, not what the country needs. Tax locks, benefit caps, and 
immigration targets are calibrated for swing seats, not for evidence. 
Coalition governments under PR must negotiate compromise — which is 
closer to honest policymaking than the artificial majorities FPTP 
produces.

**The House of Lords is unelected and indefensible.** The last three 
PMs created 120+ peers between them. The PM can "stuff unlimited 
numbers of new peers into the Lords on a whim" (ERS). This is the only 
unelected legislative chamber in a major democracy other than Canada's 
Senate. All parties except the Conservatives support some form of 
Lords reform — but Labour's proposals fall short of election.

**Trust in politics is at rock bottom.** Turnout has declined over 
decades. Voter ID requirements introduced by Conservatives have 
disenfranchised thousands without evidence of fraud. 16-17 year olds 
can vote in Scotland and Wales but not England. The system is designed 
for the convenience of parties, not citizens.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** Maintain FPTP. Maintain voter ID. Maintain 18 voting 
age. Support House of Lords as is. No constitutional reform.

**Labour:** Votes at 16. Modernisation Committee for Commons. Remove 
hereditary peers (done — passed 2025). Consult on long-term Lords 
reform. Strengthen donation rules. Address voter ID inconsistencies. 
No commitment to PR despite 2022 conference vote supporting it.

**Liberal Democrats:** PR via STV for Westminster and English local 
government. Elected House of Lords. Votes at 16. Extend franchise to 
visa-holding residents. Cap political donations. Automatic voter 
registration. Enforce Section 106 Equality Act (diversity data).

**Green:** PR for all elections. Elected second chamber. Votes at 16. 
State funding of parties to eliminate big donor dependence. Rights of 
Nature Act (constitutional innovation). Franchise extension.

**Reform UK:** Referendum on PR. Replace Lords with smaller elected 
chamber. Direct democracy elements. Term limits.

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Does FPTP produce better governance than PR?

**NO. EVIDENCE FAVOURS PR SYSTEMS.**

The ERS: countries with majoritarian systems (UK, US, Australia, 
Canada) have experienced "soaring public discontent" compared to 
PR countries. Comparative political science research consistently 
finds that PR systems produce: higher voter satisfaction, higher 
turnout, better representation of women and minorities, more 
collaborative policymaking, and policy outcomes closer to median 
voter preferences.

The standard objection — that PR produces unstable coalitions — is 
not supported cross-nationally. Germany, the Netherlands, 
Scandinavia, and New Zealand all use PR variants and have stable, 
effective governments. Italy's instability is an outlier driven by 
specific institutional features, not PR itself.

**Verdict: PR PRODUCES BETTER DEMOCRATIC OUTCOMES.** 
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Would PR change policy outcomes?

**YES. SPECIFICALLY ON THE ISSUES THIS MANIFESTO IDENTIFIES.**

Under PR, the fiscal "conspiracy of silence" becomes harder to 
maintain because coalition partners must negotiate honest 
compromises. Tax locks would be challenged by parties that need 
different revenue mixes. Environmental policy would be stronger 
(Greens would hold balance of power in most PR parliaments). 
Housing policy would be harder to block through NIMBY dynamics 
(no safe seats to protect).

The 2024 PR projection: Lab 228, Con 139, Reform 100, Green 71, 
LD ~similar. This parliament would have required Labour to 
negotiate with either Lib Dems or Greens — producing policy 
closer to the evidence base this manifesto identifies.

**Verdict: PR WOULD PRODUCE MORE EVIDENCE-ALIGNED POLICY.**
**Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH** (counterfactual, but consistent with 
comparative evidence).

### Assumption 3: Is there public support for PR?

**YES. GROWING AND NOW MAJORITY.**

YouGov (2025): 49% support PR, 26% favour FPTP. Support for FPTP 
is at an all-time low. 53% believe winning a small number of votes 
should entitle a party to at least some seats. 37% believe PR 
would make politics more accountable (vs 13-17% negative). The 
public is already voting as if the UK has PR — four parties got 
over 10% of the vote in 2024 for the first time ever.

**Verdict: PUBLIC SUPPORTS PR. Political class resists.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 4: Should the Lords be elected?

**YES. NO DEMOCRATIC JUSTIFICATION FOR APPOINTMENT.**

Every other major democracy either has an elected second chamber 
or a unicameral system. The UK Lords is the second-largest 
legislative chamber in the world (after China's NPC). Appointment 
by PM patronage is indefensible. The Lib Dem/Green position 
(elected chamber) is the only evidence-consistent one. Labour's 
hereditary peer removal was necessary but insufficient.

**Verdict: ELECTED SECOND CHAMBER.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Should 16-17 year olds vote?

**YES. EVIDENCE FROM SCOTLAND AND AUSTRIA SUPPORTS IT.**

Scotland has allowed 16-year-olds to vote since 2014 (independence 
referendum) and in all Scottish elections. Austria lowered voting 
age to 16 in 2007. Evidence shows: higher turnout among 16-17 year 
olds than 18-24 year olds (habit formation at home/school), no 
evidence of less informed voting, and improved civic education when 
linked to franchise extension.

**Verdict: EXTEND FRANCHISE TO 16.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The mechanism:

**Electoral system:**
- Replace FPTP with STV (Single Transferable Vote) for Westminster 
  — retains constituency link, proportional outcomes, multi-member 
  constituencies
- STV for English local government (Scotland has used this since 2007)
- Citizens' Assembly to design the transition (not a referendum — 
  the 2011 AV referendum was a failed process, not evidence against PR)

**House of Lords:**
- Elected second chamber using PR (different system from Commons to 
  avoid identical partisan composition)
- Transition over two parliaments: 50% elected at first election, 
  remainder at second
- Reduced size (current ~800 → ~300)
- No PM appointment power

**Franchise:**
- Votes at 16 for all UK elections
- Automatic voter registration (Wales implementing — extend UK-wide)
- Abolish or significantly expand voter ID requirements
- Extend franchise to long-term visa holders for local elections

**Standards:**
- Cap political donations at £10,000/year per person
- State funding of parties proportional to vote share
- Strengthen electoral commission independence
- Mandatory diversity data for candidates (Section 106 Equality Act)

**Devolution:**
- Genuine fiscal devolution to English regions (not just mayors)
- Subsidiarity principle: decisions at lowest effective level
- Local government funding reform (cross-domain → Taxation)

### Why this matters for every other domain:

The graph connects Electoral Reform to every domain through a 
structural edge: the policy failures identified in every section — 
the fiscal conspiracy of silence, the tax locks, the immigration 
dishonesty, the NHS underfunding, the housing failure, the 
environmental destruction — are produced by a system that rewards 
dishonesty and punishes truth-telling. PR doesn't guarantee good 
policy. But it creates a structural environment where honest 
coalition-building replaces adversarial populism.

### International precedent:

- **New Zealand:** Switched from FPTP to MMP (Mixed Member 
  Proportional) in 1996 after referendum. Government quality 
  maintained or improved. Higher Māori representation. Policy 
  outcomes more centrist and evidence-based. Most relevant 
  precedent for UK.
- **Ireland:** Uses STV. Multi-party governments. High democratic 
  satisfaction. Voters report feeling represented.
- **Germany:** MMP since 1949. Stable coalition governments. Strong 
  policy outcomes. The Greens entered government through coalition 
  — impossible under FPTP.
- **Scotland:** AMS since 1999. More proportional outcomes. Higher 
  women's representation. Multi-party parliament. Proves PR works 
  within the UK.

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. PR might empower extremes.** Under PR, Reform UK would have 
won ~100 seats in 2024. Some would argue this is a bug, not a 
feature. Counter: those voters exist and are currently unrepresented 
— their frustration is channelled into anti-system sentiment rather 
than accountable politics. Representation ≠ endorsement.

**2. Coalition government may be less decisive.** Some crises 
require rapid executive action. Coalition negotiation takes time. 
Counter: the evidence from PR countries doesn't show worse crisis 
response — and FPTP produced the Truss/Kwarteng mini-budget, the 
most disastrous economic decision in modern UK history.

**3. The 2011 AV referendum killed reform for a generation.** The 
public rejected a change to AV (which isn't PR) in 2011. This is 
used as evidence against reform. Counter: 2011 was a specific 
referendum on a specific (flawed) alternative in specific political 
circumstances. Public opinion has shifted significantly since.

**4. Labour will never support PR while FPTP benefits them.** The 
party that wins under the current system has the least incentive 
to change it. This is the structural trap: the party with power to 
reform is the party least likely to benefit from reform. The 
platform can only make the evidence visible — it can't overcome 
this incentive structure.

**5. Constitutional reform may be a distraction from substantive 
policy.** If the government has limited political capital, spending 
it on electoral reform means not spending it on NHS, housing, or 
climate. Counter: electoral reform is upstream — it changes the 
structural conditions that produce bad policy in every domain.
