# ENVIRONMENT
## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

---

## The Problem

The UK is failing its own legally binding environmental targets across 
every measurable dimension.

**Air pollution kills.** The Chief Medical Officer's 2022 report: 
outdoor air pollution in England is equivalent to 26,000-38,000 deaths 
per year. Progress on reducing key pollutants has slowed over the last 
decade. Fine particulate matter and ammonia emissions show "little to 
no change." This is a public health crisis comparable in scale to the 
NHS waiting list (cross-domain → NHS).

**Biodiversity is collapsing.** The UK is one of the most nature-
depleted countries in the world. 62% of UK seabird species are in 
decline (Seabirds Count, 2023). The drivers: land use change (71% of 
UK land is farmed), pollution, climate change, and invasive species. 
The UK committed to protect 30% of land and sea by 2030 ("30 by 30") 
under the Global Biodiversity Framework. The Office for Environmental 
Protection's 2024 report found the government is "largely off track" 
to meet Environment Act targets and other commitments.

**Water is polluted.** Sewage is one of the largest sources of water 
pollution in England. Storm overflows discharge raw sewage into rivers 
and seas. Water companies have paid billions in dividends while 
infrastructure degrades. The Environment Agency's monitoring and 
enforcement capacity has been cut. Recycling rates have stalled while 
waste generation and incineration increase.

**Farming is the nexus.** 71% of UK land is farmed. Intensive 
agricultural practices are a key driver of biodiversity decline, water 
pollution (fertiliser runoff, agricultural waste), and ammonia emissions. 
The transition from EU Common Agricultural Policy to Environmental Land 
Management (ELM) schemes — paying farmers for "public goods" — is the 
most important environmental policy lever. But the farming budget is 
insufficient and the rollout has been chaotic.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** Increase UK agriculture budget by £1bn. Ring-fence 
farming budget. Legally binding food security target. Continue ELM 
schemes. Abolish nutrient neutrality rules (to unlock 100,000 homes). 
Ratify Global Ocean Treaty. Annual oil and gas licensing rounds. 
Greenpeace score: 5/40.

**Labour:** Meet Environment Act targets. Land-use framework. Make 
ELM work for farmers and nature. National River Walks and National 
Forests. Protect green belt with "grey belt" release. Nature-friendly 
house building. Biodiversity Net Gain maintained. No new oil/gas 
exploration licenses. Greenpeace score: 21/40.

**Liberal Democrats:** Reform water companies. Plant 60 million trees/
year (exceeds CCC advice of 50m). Sewage elimination targets. Restore 
EPC C requirement for landlords. End fuel poverty. Increase regulator 
funding. Greenpeace score: 32/40.

**Green:** Rights of Nature Act (legal personhood for nature). Separate 
Climate and Nature Act. Triple farming budget for nature-friendly 
transition. End factory farming. Ban harmful pesticides (50% reduction 
by 2030). 150,000 social homes with green standards. £29bn insulation 
programme. Restore rivers. Greenpeace score: 39/40.

**Reform UK:** Scrap net zero. Fast-track oil/gas. Trial fracking. 
Explore coal mining. Scrap climate-related farming subsidies, return 
to direct payments. Stop Natural England taking enforcement action. 
Drop retained EU environmental laws. RSPB: "Nature almost entirely 
absent." Greenpeace score: not ranked (would be near zero).

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Is the UK meeting its environmental targets?

**NO. OFF TRACK ACROSS EVERY METRIC.**

The Office for Environmental Protection (2024): government is "largely 
off track" on Environment Act targets. Air quality targets, biodiversity 
targets, water quality targets, and waste reduction targets are all 
behind schedule. The Environment Act 2021 set legally binding targets 
— but targets without funding and enforcement are meaningless.

**Verdict: FAILING.** Targets exist. Delivery doesn't.
**Confidence: HIGHEST.**

### Assumption 2: Is sewage pollution getting worse?

**YES. INFRASTRUCTURE UNDERINVESTMENT IS THE CAUSE.**

Water companies have extracted billions in dividends while allowing 
infrastructure to degrade. Storm overflow discharges are increasing. 
The Environment Agency's enforcement capacity was cut during austerity 
— it can't monitor what it can't inspect. The Lib Dem proposal (reform 
water companies, ban dividends until standards met) is the most 
evidence-aligned response. Labour's approach (regulate harder) requires 
enforcement capacity that doesn't exist.

**Verdict: STRUCTURAL REFORM OF WATER COMPANIES NEEDED, not just 
regulation.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 3: Can farming be both productive and nature-friendly?

**YES, BUT TRANSITION REQUIRES SUSTAINED INVESTMENT.**

The ELM scheme (public money for public goods) is the correct principle. 
But rollout has been chaotic, payments delayed, and the budget is 
insufficient. The Green proposal (triple farming budget) is the most 
ambitious. The Conservative proposal (£1bn extra focused on food 
production) risks undermining the nature-friendly transition by 
returning to direct subsidies.

International evidence: regenerative farming can maintain yields while 
restoring soil health and biodiversity — but the transition takes 3-5 
years during which yields may dip. Farmers need financial support 
through the transition.

**Verdict: ELM IS CORRECT. Budget needs doubling minimum.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 4: Should nutrient neutrality rules be abolished?

**NO. THEY EXIST BECAUSE WATERWAYS ARE POLLUTED.**

The Conservative proposal to abolish nutrient neutrality (to unlock 
100,000 homes) removes the protection without fixing the pollution. 
Nutrient neutrality ensures new developments don't add more pollution 
to already-stressed waterways. The right response is to fix the 
pollution source (agricultural runoff, sewage), not remove the 
protection from the consequence.

**Verdict: FIX THE POLLUTION, DON'T REMOVE THE PROTECTION.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Is Reform's proposal to scrap environmental protections viable?

**CATASTROPHIC FOR NATURE AND LEGALLY QUESTIONABLE.**

Reform proposes dropping retained EU environmental laws (including 
Habitats Regulations), stopping Natural England enforcement, and 
scrapping nature-friendly farming schemes. The RSPB: this would 
"give free reign to those who want to pollute or exploit precious 
natural spaces." The UK has legally binding international commitments 
(Convention on Biological Diversity, Paris Agreement) that would be 
violated. The Green Finance Institute estimates continued biodiversity 
decline could cause a UK economic shock comparable to the 2008 
financial crisis.

**Verdict: NOT VIABLE. Economically, legally, and ecologically 
destructive.** **Confidence: HIGHEST.**

### Assumption 6: What is the economic value of nature?

**ENORMOUS AND SYSTEMATICALLY UNDERPRICED.**

The Dasgupta Review (2021, commissioned by HM Treasury) concluded that 
the global economy is embedded in nature, not external to it. Nature 
provides services (pollination, water purification, flood defence, 
carbon sequestration, soil formation) worth trillions globally. The 
UK's natural capital accounts show declining asset values across 
freshwater, air quality, and biodiversity. The Green Finance Institute 
warns that biodiversity decline poses systemic financial risk.

Pollination alone is worth ~£700m/year to UK agriculture. Peatlands 
store ~3.2 billion tonnes of carbon. Healthy soils prevent flooding. 
Nature-based solutions are often cheaper than engineered alternatives.

**Verdict: NATURE IS INFRASTRUCTURE. Destroying it is economically 
irrational.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The mechanism:

**Water:**
- Structural reform of water companies: public interest mandate, 
  dividend ban until infrastructure standards met, automatic 
  penalties for sewage spills
- Restore Environment Agency enforcement capacity to 2010 levels
- Legally binding sewage elimination targets for designated areas 
  by 2030 (Lib Dem)
- Implement polluter-pays for agricultural runoff

**Biodiversity:**
- Deliver 30 by 30 commitment (30% land and sea protected by 2030)
- Expand and properly fund protected area network
- Biodiversity Net Gain enforced robustly in planning
- Nature recovery networks connecting fragmented habitats
- Marine protected areas: ban industrial fishing in MPAs

**Farming:**
- Double ELM budget to support nature-friendly transition
- Maintain public money for public goods principle
- Support regenerative farming transition (3-5 year bridging finance)
- Reduce pesticide use 50% by 2030 (Green/EU target)
- Local food procurement in public institutions (50%+ target)

**Air quality:**
- Clean Air Act: legally binding WHO air quality limits
- Expand clean air zones in cities
- Address ammonia from agriculture (largest remaining source)
- Invest in monitoring network

**Waste:**
- Extended Producer Responsibility (make producers pay for packaging 
  waste)
- Deposit Return Scheme for drinks containers
- Binding recycling rate targets (currently stalled)
- Reduce incineration through upstream waste reduction

### International precedent:

- **Costa Rica:** Doubled forest cover in 30 years through payment 
  for ecosystem services. Proves nature recovery at national scale 
  is possible when funded.
- **Netherlands:** Leading in circular agriculture and nature-inclusive 
  farming — though politically contentious (farmer protests).
- **EU Nature Restoration Law (2024):** Legally binding restoration 
  targets for all member states. The UK helped design this but 
  Brexit means it doesn't apply.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- ELM budget doubling: ~£3bn/yr additional
- Water infrastructure: £10bn+ over 10 years (from water company 
  investment, not public spending)
- Environment Agency restoration: ~£500m/yr
- Air quality: ~£500m/yr
- Total public spending: ~£4-5bn/yr additional

**Benefits:**
- Air pollution health costs: PHE estimates ~£20bn/yr in health 
  and productivity losses from air pollution
- Flood defence: nature-based solutions 50-75% cheaper than 
  engineered defences
- Pollination: ~£700m/yr to UK agriculture
- Carbon: peatland restoration worth billions in sequestration
- Water treatment: healthy catchments reduce water treatment costs
- Tourism: UK natural environment supports £billions in rural economy

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. Nature-friendly farming may reduce food output.** The transition 
period could reduce domestic food production. In a world of food price 
shocks and supply chain disruption, this is a legitimate concern. 
Counter: regenerative farming maintains long-term yields while reducing 
input costs.

**2. The economic case may not convince.** Despite the Dasgupta Review, 
Treasury continues to undervalue natural capital. If the fiscal pressure 
from NHS/Defence/Housing dominates, environment loses. The graph shows 
this: environment competes for the same constrained fiscal space.

**3. Public may prioritise cost of living over environment.** If energy 
bills and food costs dominate public concern, environmental regulation 
may be perceived as a luxury. Counter: pollution and biodiversity 
decline increase costs over time (health, food, flooding).
