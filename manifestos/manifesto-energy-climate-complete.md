## The Problem

The UK's energy system is broken in three measurable ways.

**Cost.** Typical household energy bills are 35% higher than pre-crisis levels and 
will remain elevated for the foreseeable future. The Q3 2025 price cap sits at 
£1,720 for typical dual fuel consumption. The UK has the second-highest domestic 
electricity prices among IEA countries (DESNZ Quarterly Energy Prices, Sep 2025). 
The primary driver of high bills is wholesale gas prices — because the UK's marginal 
pricing system means gas sets the electricity price even when most generation is 
renewable.

**Security.** The UK imports a significant and volatile share of its energy. The 
2022 crisis — triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine — demonstrated that 
dependence on international gas markets exposes the UK to geopolitical shocks 
entirely outside its control. Bills spiked because gas prices spiked, not because 
of any domestic supply failure.

**Emissions.** UK electricity generation carbon intensity stands at approximately 
154g CO2/kWh (DESNZ, 2025). The legally binding target is net zero by 2050, 
requiring this to fall below 50g CO2/kWh. Clean sources currently provide ~64% 
of generation. Progress is real but insufficient at the current pace: the Climate 
Change Committee has repeatedly found the UK off-track on its carbon budgets.

These three problems — cost, security, emissions — are interconnected. The energy 
system that solves all three simultaneously is one that generates most electricity 
domestically from sources with zero fuel cost and zero carbon emissions. The 
question is not whether to transition, but how fast, at what cost, and with what 
generation mix.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** Maintain net zero 2050 but take a "pragmatic" approach. Annual 
North Sea oil and gas licensing. New gas power stations. Two SMR fleets within 100 
days. Complete Hinkley Point C, Sizewell C, build new plant at Wylfa. Treble 
offshore wind but restrict onshore wind and agricultural solar. Reduce green levies. 
£6bn home insulation programme. Parliamentary vote before adopting new carbon targets.

**Labour:** Clean power by 2030 (decarbonised grid). Create Great British Energy 
(public energy company). Double onshore wind, triple solar, quadruple offshore wind. 
New nuclear including SMRs. No new North Sea licences. Windfall tax on oil and gas. 
National Wealth Fund for green co-investment. Double home insulation funding. Major 
planning reform.

**Liberal Democrats:** Net zero by 2045. Invest in renewables and nuclear. Emergency 
home insulation programme. Ban fracking and new coal. Community energy. Windfall tax 
on oil and gas. Reform financial regulation for Paris compliance. Invest in tidal, 
wave, hydrogen, pumped storage, battery.

**Green:** Carbon neutral before 2040. Phase out nuclear entirely. 70% electricity 
from wind by 2030. Cancel existing fossil fuel licences. £40bn/year green investment 
funded by wealth taxes. Remove all subsidies for fossil fuels and nuclear. 
Insulation-first approach. Community-owned local energy.

**Reform UK:** Scrap net zero entirely. Scrap all renewable subsidies. Fast-track 
North Sea oil and gas. Enable fracking on trial. Fast-track SMRs. Scrap VAT on 
energy. Explore coal mining. Claims CO2 reduction "cannot help" climate change.

## What The Evidence Says

Ten key assumptions were tested against the best available evidence. Sources include 
IEA, IPCC, NESO, OBR, CCC, BGS, Lazard, IRENA, IFS, and peer-reviewed academic 
literature. Full evidence documentation with source tagging is available in the 
companion evidence files.

**1. Nuclear vs renewables cost.** Renewables are cheaper per unit: UK CfD auction 
prices show solar at £61/MWh, onshore wind at £64, offshore wind at £73, versus 
Hinkley Point C's strike price of £92.50 (all 2012 prices). However, LCOE 
comparisons are incomplete — system integration costs for renewables add 
$25-30/MWh at high penetration. The IEA/NEA finds nuclear is the lowest-cost 
dispatchable low-carbon technology, and extending existing nuclear plants is the 
cheapest electricity source of any kind. **Confidence: HIGH.**

**2. Can the UK grid decarbonise by 2030?** NESO's independent assessment (Nov 2024) 
concludes it is "achievable" but a "huge challenge" requiring "perfect execution." 
£40bn annual investment needed — 4x current rate. 1,000km+ of new power lines. 
Offshore wind must grow from 15GW to ~45GW in 5 years (current build rate: <2GW/year). 
Aurora modelling suggests the UK is on track for 100% clean power by 2051 at current 
pace. **Confidence: MEDIUM.** Technically possible, delivery risk is high.

**3. Is fracking viable in UK?** The BGS (2022) found earthquake prediction 
"remains a scientific challenge," UK resource estimates were revised down 83-94%, 
both UK fracking attempts caused seismic events requiring suspension, expert 
consensus is that UK geology is unsuitable, and only 17% of the public supports 
it. **Confidence: HIGH.** Not viable.

**4. Are renewables subsidy-dependent?** Currently, offshore wind receives 52% of 
revenue from CfD subsidy. But CfD strike prices have fallen 68% in seven years 
(AR1 to AR4). Older expensive schemes (RO, FiT) are expiring. Solar is approaching 
subsidy-free. Subsidy-free projects already exist (~4GW pipeline). The CfD mechanism 
also protects consumers when prices spike — generators paid back £346m in 2022. 
Scrapping subsidies would not save money; it would remove investor certainty and 
likely raise bills. **Confidence: HIGH.**

**5. New North Sea extraction and net zero.** The IEA's Net Zero by 2050 pathway 
states: "no new oil and gas fields approved for development." The IPCC, CCC, and 
Nature all agree. The CCC says UK reserves are "too small to impact meaningfully 
the prices faced by UK consumers." UK gas trades on international markets — 
domestic production doesn't stay domestic. **Confidence: HIGH.**

**6. Is CO2 reduction futile?** No. 99.9% of peer-reviewed scientific papers agree 
on human-caused climate change (Lynas et al., 2021). The IPCC AR6 states it is 
"unequivocal." This is not contested science. **Confidence: HIGHEST POSSIBLE.**

**7. Employment impact.** Renewables employed ~247,000 in UK in 2023 (growing). 
Oil and gas employ ~200,000 (declining). Net employment impact of transition is 
positive in aggregate, but geographic and skill mismatch is real. No party has 
a credible place-based transition plan. **Confidence: MEDIUM.**

**8. Denmark precedent.** 50 years of cross-party wind policy consensus. ~60% of 
electricity from wind (2023). Created world-leading export industry (Vestas, 
Ørsted). Key lessons: community ownership builds consent; industrial strategy must 
accompany energy policy; grid interconnection essential; long-term consistency 
matters more than any single policy.

**9. Germany cautionary tale.** Phased out nuclear while decarbonising. Result: 
electricity prices doubled, CO2 fell only ~2%/year, nuclear gap filled by coal 
and gas. PwC estimates emission-free generation would have been 94% in 2024 
(instead of 61%) if nuclear had been maintained. Cost to consumers: $12bn/year 
(Berkeley). The single clearest lesson in global energy policy: do not close 
nuclear while trying to decarbonise.

**10. France nuclear success.** Built 56 standardised reactors from 1974. Achieved 
~55g CO2/kWh electricity (vs UK 154g, Germany ~350g). Exports ~€3bn/year in 
electricity. Proves nuclear can deliver low-carbon baseload at scale — but 
France's aging fleet and Flamanville's cost overruns show replication is hard.

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The policy:

A domestic clean power system built on rapid renewable deployment, maintained 
nuclear baseload, grid-first infrastructure investment, and demand reduction 
through insulation — delivered within a realistic 2030-2035 window.

### The mechanism:

**Generation:**
- Accelerate offshore wind deployment toward 40-50GW by early 2030s through 
  reformed planning and CfD auctions
- Remove planning restrictions on onshore wind and solar immediately
- Maintain all existing nuclear plants to end of safe operational life (extend 
  where possible)
- Pursue SMRs using standardised designs only — do not commit to bespoke 
  large reactors (Hinkley lesson)
- No new fossil fuel exploration licences — existing production continues 
  during transition
- No fracking
- Invest in tidal, wave, and long-duration storage (pumped hydro, LAES, 
  compressed air)

**Grid:**
- Grid investment must LEAD generation, not follow it — this is the single 
  highest infrastructure priority
- Reform connections queue (implement NESO recommendations)
- Build 1,000km+ of new transmission lines by 2030
- Introduce locational marginal pricing (could save £30bn by 2035 per 
  Grantham Institute estimate)

**Demand:**
- Emergency home insulation programme: minimum £3bn/year
- This is the cheapest "generation" available — energy not used is energy 
  not generated
- Target worst-performing housing stock first (EPC D and below)
- Community energy schemes for local ownership and engagement

**Market:**
- Maintain CfD mechanism (proven, cost-declining, protects consumers in spikes)
- Reform wholesale pricing to decouple electricity price from gas price — 
  the marginal pricing system means consumers don't benefit from cheap 
  renewables while gas sets the price
- Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism to prevent industrial carbon leakage

### The assumptions (explicitly stated):

1. Renewable costs continue declining or stabilise at current levels
2. Grid buildout achieves unprecedented but not physically impossible pace
3. Nuclear fleet life extensions are technically feasible and safe
4. SMR designs achieve regulatory approval and cost targets (UNPROVEN)
5. Battery storage costs continue declining (current trend: -89% since 2010)
6. Public consent for infrastructure (pylons, substations, wind farms) can 
   be maintained through community benefit and ownership
7. Wholesale market reform is politically achievable
8. Gas backup capacity maintained for security (up to 5% of generation)

### International precedent:

- **Denmark:** Wind-dominant system works at 60%+ of generation. Community 
  ownership model for public consent. Export industry created alongside energy 
  transition.
- **France:** Nuclear baseload achieves lowest-carbon electricity in Europe. 
  Standardised design is critical to cost control.
- **Germany:** DO NOT phase out nuclear while decarbonising. This is the 
  most expensive and least effective approach possible.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- £40bn/year investment required (NESO estimate) — 4x current rate
- Equivalent to >1% of GDP
- Grid infrastructure: £55bn+ by 2030 (transmission alone)
- Home insulation: £3bn+/year
- Transition support for fossil fuel communities: £TBD (evidence gap)

**Hard benefits:**
- Reduced fossil fuel imports: UK currently spends ~£50bn/year on energy 
  imports (volatile with gas price)
- A domestic clean system converts perpetual fuel spending into asset 
  investment (house vs rent analogy)
- CfD generators paid back £346m in 2022 crisis — system acts as price 
  insurance
- Reduced wholesale prices: each 1% increase in renewable share reduces 
  wholesale price (ECIU analysis)
- Locational pricing reform: potential £30bn saving by 2035

**Proxy-monetised benefits (WELLBY/QALY methodology where applicable):**
- Reduced air pollution: fewer fossil fuel emissions = fewer respiratory 
  and cardiovascular deaths. Monetisable via QALY/DALY values.
- Energy security premium: reduced exposure to geopolitical shocks. 
  The 2022 crisis cost UK households ~£40bn in excess bills above 
  pre-crisis levels — this is the counterfactual cost of inaction.
- Climate damage avoided: Social Cost of Carbon estimates range from 
  $50-200/tonne CO2 (contested but directionally clear).

**Unmonetised benefits (stated, not priced):**
- Industrial strategy: clean energy supply chain creates exportable 
  capabilities (Denmark model)
- Democratic energy: community ownership shifts power from 
  multinational utilities to local communities
- Intergenerational equity: avoiding climate tipping points has 
  incalculable long-term value

**Sensitivity analysis:**
- If renewable costs rise 20%: position still holds (still cheaper than 
  new nuclear or gas with carbon price)
- If SMRs fail to deliver: position still holds (more renewables + storage 
  fills gap, at higher system cost)
- If grid buildout is 2 years late: 2032-2033 delivery instead of 2030 — 
  direction unchanged, timeline shifts
- If gas prices spike again: case for transition STRENGTHENS (domestic 
  generation insulates from shocks)
- If gas prices collapse: case weakens on pure cost basis but holds on 
  emissions and security grounds

### Evidence gaps (what we don't know):

1. SMR delivered costs in UK (no data — first projects not operational)
2. Long-duration storage costs at scale (uncertain, critical for 100% 
   renewables scenario)
3. Precise employment transition impacts by region (aggregate positive, 
   local distribution unknown)
4. Public consent dynamics for infrastructure at required scale
5. Optimal share of nuclear vs storage in 2035+ system (modelling uncertain)
6. Impact of AI-driven electricity demand growth on supply requirements

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**The strongest counter-arguments:**

1. **The pace assumption may be unrealistic.** NESO says "perfect execution" 
   is required. The UK has never built infrastructure at this pace in 
   peacetime. Planning objections, supply chain constraints, and grid queue 
   delays could push delivery well past 2035. If this happens, the UK faces 
   a gap that only gas (or imported electricity) can fill.

2. **System costs may be higher than projected.** As renewable penetration 
   increases beyond 60-70%, balancing and curtailment costs rise non-linearly. 
   The LCOE advantage of renewables narrows significantly when full system 
   costs are included. A 2025 UNECE report warns that LCOE alone "risks 
   underestimating true system costs."

3. **Nuclear may be more necessary than I've stated.** If storage technology 
   doesn't deliver at scale and cost, nuclear baseload becomes not just 
   useful but essential. France's low-carbon grid is built on nuclear, 
   not storage. The anti-nuclear position (GRN) and the nuclear-cautious 
   position (this document) both carry risk if storage disappoints.

4. **Consumer costs during transition may be higher than stated.** Even 
   if long-run costs fall, the transition period involves simultaneous 
   investment in new capacity while maintaining old capacity. This 
   double-running period is expensive and falls on current billpayers.

5. **The political economy may not support this.** A 2030-2035 transition 
   requires sustained cross-party commitment across at least 2-3 electoral 
   cycles. Denmark achieved this. The UK has not demonstrated this capacity 
   on any major infrastructure programme in living memory.

**What evidence would change my mind:**

- If SMRs deliver at <£60/MWh reliably, the case for more new nuclear 
  strengthens significantly
- If long-duration storage fails to scale below £100/MWh, the case for 
  nuclear baseload becomes essential rather than helpful
- If renewable costs reverse their decline and rise sustainably above 
  gas+CCS, the generation mix needs rethinking
- If a credible CBA showed fracking could deliver meaningful UK gas 
  at lower social cost than alternatives, the ban should be reconsidered 
  (current evidence strongly says it can't)

## What Else Should Be Done
### Innovations no party is adequately proposing

### 1. Wholesale Market Reform: Break the Gas-Price Link
- **What:** Reform marginal pricing so cheap renewable electricity actually 
  reaches consumers as cheap bills. Either via a single-buyer model (NESO 
  purchases low-carbon power at fixed prices) or accelerated CfD coverage 
  to naturally displace gas from price-setting.
- **Why:** The single biggest reason UK bills are high despite cheap renewables. 
  Gas sets the electricity price ~80% of the time despite providing ~25% of 
  generation. Consumers pay gas prices for wind power.
- **Evidence:** Common Wealth think tank estimates decoupling could save 
  households up to £203/year. Gas plants made ~£3bn in excess profits in 
  2022 from the pricing gap (UCL research). Spain's renewable expansion has 
  already significantly reduced the gas price-setting frequency. The UK 
  government's own REMA review spent 3 years examining this but concluded 
  in July 2025 to retain national pricing with reforms rather than fundamental 
  restructuring. Labour is now exploring decoupling (as of June 2025 Hansard debate).
- **Precedent:** Spain — strong solar and wind growth has materially reduced 
  gas influence on wholesale prices. UK wholesale prices are already higher 
  than Spain's despite similar renewable shares, partly due to market design.
- **Estimated impact:** £200+/household/year bill reduction if fully implemented. 
  Removes the structural absurdity where building more renewables doesn't 
  lower bills because gas still sets the price.
- **Risk:** Market redesign could unsettle investor confidence during the 
  critical buildout period. Energy UK argues marginal pricing is 
  self-correcting as renewables grow. Transition design is complex.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. The problem is real and measured. The solution 
  is debated among credible experts. The government is actively exploring 
  it but has not committed.

### 2. Mandatory Community Benefit & Ownership for All New Energy Infrastructure
- **What:** Require all new wind, solar, and transmission projects to offer 
  a minimum community benefit package: discounted local electricity, community 
  ownership stake (5-10%), and local employment commitments.
- **Why:** The single biggest constraint on delivery pace is planning consent 
  and public opposition. Denmark solved this through community ownership — 
  when communities profit from infrastructure, they support it. The UK has 
  no equivalent requirement.
- **Evidence:** Denmark's community wind ownership model created public 
  consent that sustained 50 years of policy. NESO's Clean Power report 
  identifies public consent as a critical risk. The government's own 
  community benefits framework for onshore wind is voluntary and weak.
  Scotland's community energy sector is more developed than England's and 
  has higher public support for wind development.
- **Precedent:** Denmark (community ownership standard since 1980s). 
  Germany (Bürgerenergie — citizen energy cooperatives). Scotland 
  (Community Energy Scotland programme).
- **Estimated impact:** Reduced planning objections → faster buildout → 
  earlier delivery of clean power target. Direct financial benefit to 
  communities hosting infrastructure. Hard to monetise precisely but 
  the Danish precedent suggests this is the difference between 
  5-year and 15-year delivery timelines.
- **Risk:** Increases project complexity and cost marginally. Developers 
  may resist mandatory requirements. Legal design of ownership structures 
  needs careful work.
- **Confidence:** HIGH that it improves consent. MODERATE on exact mechanism.

### 3. Energy Efficiency as Infrastructure: National Insulation Service
- **What:** Create a permanent, scaled public insulation service — not a 
  subsidy scheme but a delivery body. Government directly employs or 
  contracts insulation installers at scale, targets worst-performing 
  housing stock (EPC D and below) with area-based rollout.
- **Why:** Every party proposes insulation funding, but delivery is the 
  bottleneck, not funding. Previous schemes (Green Homes Grant) failed 
  because they relied on fragmented private contractors and household 
  applications. Germany's KfW bank model shows that institutional delivery 
  bodies outperform voucher schemes. Insulation is the highest-ROI energy 
  investment: cheaper than any generation source because it reduces demand 
  permanently.
- **Evidence:** The Green Homes Grant (2020-2021) was scrapped after 
  delivering only 10% of target installations due to contractor shortages 
  and scheme complexity. NAO found it was "poor value for money." 
  Germany's KfW programme has retrofitted millions of homes through 
  institutional delivery. The CCC has repeatedly identified insulation 
  as the most cost-effective carbon abatement measure, at negative 
  cost (saves more than it costs over building lifetime).
- **Precedent:** Germany (KfW). Netherlands (Energiesprong — whole-house 
  retrofit in <2 weeks using prefabricated insulation). 
  Kirklees Council's free insulation programme (2007-2010) insulated 
  51,000 homes using area-based delivery.
- **Estimated impact:** Each home retrofitted saves £300-600/year in bills. 
  At 1m homes/year: £300-600m/year in consumer savings. Reduces peak 
  demand, reducing need for gas peaking plants. Creates 50,000+ skilled 
  jobs in construction/retrofit sector. Long-term: permanent demand 
  reduction worth more than any supply-side investment.
- **Risk:** Upfront cost is significant (~£10bn/year at scale). 
  Workforce doesn't exist yet — needs training pipeline. Social housing 
  is easier than private (access and consent issues).
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Evidence base is deep, international precedent is 
  strong, and every credible analysis identifies this as the most 
  cost-effective intervention available.

### 4. Strategic Electricity Reserve: Redesign Gas Role
- **What:** Rather than gas plants operating commercially in the wholesale 
  market (profiting from high marginal prices), move gas capacity into a 
  strategic reserve. Maintain full gas capacity for security but operate 
  it only on dispatch by NESO when renewables can't meet demand. Pay 
  availability fees rather than market prices.
- **Why:** Currently gas plants earn windfall profits from the marginal 
  pricing system even when only providing a small share of generation. 
  A strategic reserve maintains security while removing the price-setting 
  distortion. This is how many countries handle military reserves — you 
  don't pay soldiers combat wages while they're in barracks.
- **Evidence:** Common Wealth's single-buyer proposal includes moving gas 
  to strategic reserve. Greenpeace has proposed a similar model. The 
  concept is consistent with NESO's Clean Power definition (gas as 
  backup, not baseload). Capacity Market already pays availability 
  fees — this extends the principle.
- **Precedent:** Partial — Capacity Market is a weak version. Belgium's 
  Strategic Reserve mechanism (2014-) pays for availability of non-market 
  generation. Australia's Reliability and Emergency Reserve Trader (RERT).
- **Estimated impact:** Removes gas from price-setting → lower wholesale 
  prices. Maintains 100% of security of supply. Reduces windfall profits 
  to gas generators. Exact savings depend on gas price trajectory.
- **Risk:** Complex to implement. Gas generators would resist losing market 
  revenue. Availability fee level must be high enough to maintain capacity.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Economically sound in principle, 
  implementation complexity is high.

### 5. Publicly Funded Pilot Programme for Evidence Gaps
- **What:** Allocate £500m over 5 years specifically to resolve the 
  evidence gaps identified in this document: SMR delivered costs, 
  long-duration storage at scale, regional employment transition 
  impacts, optimal nuclear share modelling.
- **Why:** Several critical policy decisions are blocked by evidence gaps. 
  Rather than arguing ideologically about nuclear vs storage, fund 
  the research that resolves the question. The platform this manifesto 
  represents is built on the principle that better evidence → better 
  policy. This is putting money where the methodology is.
- **Evidence:** The NAO found only 8% of UK government programme 
  expenditure was subject to meaningful impact evaluation. The UK 
  spends billions on energy policy without funding the research to 
  know which investments work best. £500m is <1.5% of annual energy 
  investment and would pay for itself if it improves allocation of 
  the other 98.5%.
- **Precedent:** Finland's UBI pilot (structured policy experiment 
  with published results). Denmark's energy island programme (R&D 
  integrated into infrastructure). ARPA-E (US) for energy technology 
  moonshots.
- **Estimated impact:** Resolves key uncertainties that currently 
  force ideological rather than evidence-based decisions. If it 
  identifies even one major misallocation in the £40bn/year 
  investment programme, the ROI is enormous.
- **Risk:** Results may not arrive fast enough for political 
  timescales. Research findings might be politically inconvenient 
  (that's the point).
- **Confidence:** HIGH. The principle of funding evidence to improve 
  policy decisions is uncontroversial. The specific allocation 
  needs detailed scoping.

## Emerging Science & Technology: Where to Invest for the Next Decade

The evidence-based position above is built on current technology. But the 
energy landscape in 2035 will include technologies currently in late R&D 
or early commercialisation. A credible strategy must invest in these now. 
The UK has genuine world-class capability in several — the question is 
whether we invest at scale to capture the industrial benefit.

### FUSION ENERGY
**Status:** $10bn+ private investment since 2021. First PPAs signed 
(Helion/Microsoft 50MW, CFS/Google 200MW). Commercial plants targeted 
for mid-2030s.

**UK position:** World-class. UKAEA's STEP programme at West Burton 
targeting pilot plant early 2030s. Tokamak Energy achieved first HTS 
fusion magnet replicating power plant fields (Nov 2025). £45m Sunrise 
AI supercomputer operational June 2026. UK published 2026 Fusion 
Strategy focused on commercialisation. The Oxford corridor is a 
global fusion hub.

**Investment case:** £2-3bn/year could secure UK as a major fusion 
economy. The IP, workforce, and institutional knowledge exist. 
US and Chinese investment risks outpacing UK. Market projected 
>$350bn by 2050.

**Confidence:** MODERATE-HIGH that fusion works within 15 years. 
HIGH that UK should invest given existing advantage.

### SODIUM-ION BATTERIES
**Status:** MIT 2026 Breakthrough Technology. Entering commercial 
deployment. CATL shipping cells. BYD residential products expected 
2026-2027.

**UK position:** Batri (Wales) + Swansea University produced Europe's 
first sodium-ion cell with fully UK-manufactured components (2025). 
Uses Welsh anthracite-derived carbon anodes — genuinely sovereign 
battery capability.

**Performance:** 150-160 Wh/kg (comparable to LFP lithium-ion). 
3,000-4,000 cycles. Better cold weather performance. 40-60% cheaper 
raw materials. Ideal for grid-scale storage where size matters less 
than cost per cycle.

**Investment case:** UK has research base AND raw materials. Could 
build European sodium-ion manufacturing hub without dependency on 
Chinese lithium supply chains. This is an industrial sovereignty 
opportunity, not just an energy one.

**Confidence:** HIGH that technology works. MODERATE on UK capturing 
industrial benefit before others.

### LONG-DURATION ENERGY STORAGE (Flow Batteries, LAES, Compressed Air)
**Status:** Critical gap. Multiple chemistries maturing. UK cap-and-floor 
funding mechanism expected 2026.

**Why this matters most:** This is THE technology that determines whether 
100% renewables works without nuclear baseload. If flow batteries 
deliver at <£100/MWh for 8-24 hour storage, the nuclear debate shifts 
fundamentally. If they don't, nuclear becomes essential.

**UK position:** Cambridge startup claiming electrolytes tripling flow 
battery energy density. NESO identifies LDES as critical gap. Pumped 
hydro projects (7GW with planning) need acceleration.

**Investment case:** HIGHEST PRIORITY of any emerging technology. 
Resolves the central evidence gap in the entire energy domain.

**Confidence:** HIGH that technology works. UNCERTAIN on cost at scale.

### VEHICLE-TO-GRID (V2G)
**Status:** Early deployment. Average UK EV battery (40-80 kWh) can 
power a home for 2-5 days.

**What to do:** Mandate bidirectional charging in new EVs post-2028. 
Subsidise smart home chargers. Create aggregator framework. NESO 
assumes 275,000 EVs participating by 2030.

**Investment case:** Cheapest grid flexibility available — the batteries 
already exist in people's driveways.

**Confidence:** HIGH on technology. MODERATE on uptake.

### PEROVSKITE SOLAR
**Status:** Approaching commercial deployment. Oxford PV (UK) leading 
globally.

**What:** Layer on existing silicon cells, boosting efficiency from 
~22% to ~33%. Can be printed on flexible surfaces. UK-origin 
technology with major export potential.

**Investment case:** Support Oxford PV's manufacturing scale-up. 
UK has a genuine first-mover advantage. Don't let it move abroad.

**Confidence:** HIGH. Technology proven. Durability testing ongoing.

### HIGH-TEMPERATURE SUPERCONDUCTORS
**Status:** Tokamak Energy's Nov 2025 breakthrough. Applications in 
fusion magnets and potentially lossless grid transmission.

**Investment case:** Dual-use with fusion programme. HTS cables 
could reduce new pylon construction needed (addressing public 
consent bottleneck). Maintain through fusion investment.

**Confidence:** HIGH for fusion. MODERATE for grid applications.

### ENHANCED GEOTHERMAL
**Status:** US proving commercial viability (Fervo Energy). UK has 
one pilot (United Downs, Cornwall).

**What:** Drill deep, fracture hot rock, circulate water for steam. 
Works almost anywhere — not just volcanic regions. 24/7 clean 
baseload heat and power. Similar drilling technique to fracking 
but extracting heat, not gas.

**Investment case:** If viable at UK scale, provides the baseload 
clean power the nuclear debate is about — without cost overruns 
or waste. Needs BGS assessment of UK-wide potential + 2-3 
additional pilot sites.

**Confidence:** MODERATE. US evidence promising. UK-specific 
assessment needed.

### Investment Priority Summary

| Technology | UK Advantage | Timeline | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Long-duration storage | Emerging | 2028-2032 | HIGHEST |
| Fusion | World-class | 2035-2040 | HIGH |
| Sodium-ion batteries | Strong | 2027-2030 | HIGH |
| Perovskite solar | World-class | 2026-2028 | HIGH |
| V2G infrastructure | Needs building | 2028-2030 | MEDIUM-HIGH |
| Enhanced geothermal | Minimal but suitable | 2030-2035 | MEDIUM |
| HTS superconductors | Strong (via fusion) | 2030+ | MEDIUM |
| Solid-state batteries | Research only | 2030-2035 | MEDIUM |

Total additional investment: ~£2-3bn/year — less than 10% of the 
£40bn/year core transition. Any one of these could fundamentally 
alter the cost, reliability, or architecture of the UK's clean 
energy system.
