## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

## The Problem

The UK's fiscal position is defined by a fundamental dishonesty that 
every party participates in. Three measurable dimensions:

**Taxes are near historic highs and still insufficient.** The UK tax 
burden is at its highest sustained level since the late 1940s. Yet 
public services are visibly failing (NHS, social care, housing, local 
government). Debt stands at its highest level in over 60 years. Debt 
interest alone costs ~£50bn/year more than forecast. Despite record 
tax levels, spending on many public services will need to be cut or 
taxes raised further just to prevent debt ratcheting upward (IFS).

**Every major party has locked itself out of the tools that could fix 
this.** Income tax, National Insurance, and VAT together account for 
two-thirds of all tax revenue. Both Conservative and Labour pledged 
not to raise any of them. The IFS describes this as a "tax lock arms 
race" and calls it "a mistake." Labour's promise of "no tax increases 
on working people" effectively rules out all tax rises — there is no 
tax paid exclusively by non-workers. The result: every manifesto 
implies either spending cuts they won't name or tax rises they've 
promised not to make.

**Fiscal drag is the hidden tax rise nobody admits to.** Frozen income 
tax and NI thresholds — maintained by both Conservative and Labour 
governments — drag more people into higher tax brackets as wages rise. 
The OBR estimates threshold freezes will raise ~£41bn/year by 2028-29. 
This is approximately four times the value of the 2p NI cut the 
Conservatives campaigned on. Both parties are raising taxes on working 
people through the back door while promising not to raise them through 
the front door.

The IFS diagnosis: "Manifestos leave voters guessing over policy on 
tax and spending." The choice facing any government is a trilemma: 
raise taxes, cut spending, or borrow more. No manifesto honestly 
addresses this.

**Labour:** No rises to IT rates, NI, VAT, corporation tax. VAT on 
private schools (£1.7bn/yr). Abolish non-dom status. Close tax

Business tax roadmap for stability. One fiscal event per year. 
Fund spending through "growth."

**Liberal Democrats:** Reverse bank levy/surcharge cuts (£8.7bn by

**Green:** Wealth tax: 1% on assets above £10m, 2% above £1bn. 
Equalise CGT with income tax. Reform IHT. Restrict pension tax relief

free personal care. Massive net tax-raising agenda.

**Reform UK:** £50bn/yr spending cuts. Abolish IHT under £2m. Raise 
IT threshold to £20,000. Cut corporation tax. Cut fuel duty. Scrap 
VAT on energy. Employer NI surcharge on foreign workers. Fund through 
spending cuts and "scrapping net zero."

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Can tax cuts pay for themselves through growth?

**NO CREDIBLE EVIDENCE AT UK TAX LEVELS.**

The Laffer curve (tax cuts → growth → more revenue) has never been 
demonstrated to work at the UK's current tax rates, which are well 
below the theoretical revenue-maximising rate. The IFS consistently 
finds that tax cuts reduce revenue. The Truss/Kwarteng mini-budget 
(September 2022) demonstrated what happens when markets don't believe 
unfunded tax cuts will generate growth.

**Verdict: NOT SUPPORTED.** Tax cuts at current UK rates reduce revenue.
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Can the tax gap close enough to fund spending?

**PARTIALLY. EVERY PARTY OVERSTATES IT.**

The total UK tax gap is ~£36bn (HMRC estimate). Both Labour (£5bn) 
and Lib Dems (£7.2bn) claim significant revenue from closing it. The 
IFS notes that HMRC's largest tax gap is from small businesses, not 
large corporations. Closing the gap requires investment in HMRC 
capacity, not just "political will." Every government promises to 
close it; none has closed more than a fraction.

**Verdict: CAN RAISE SOME REVENUE but nowhere near the scale claimed.**
**Confidence: MEDIUM.**

### Assumption 3: Is a wealth tax feasible?

**ECONOMICALLY SOUND. POLITICALLY AND ADMINISTRATIVELY CHALLENGING.**

The Green proposal (1% above £10m, 2% above £1bn) has 75% public 
support (YouGov 2025). The IFS argues that fixing existing wealth-
related taxes (council tax, CGT, IHT) would be more effective than 
a new annual wealth tax. Challenges: valuation of illiquid assets 
(businesses, land, art), capital flight risk, administrative 
complexity.

The Wealth Tax Commission (LSE/Warwick, 2020) concluded a one-off 
wealth tax was feasible but an annual wealth tax would face significant 
evasion and administrative challenges. Norway, Spain, and Switzerland 
have annual wealth taxes — Norway's has faced criticism for driving 
capital flight. Switzerland's works because rates are very low (0.1-0.5%).

**Verdict: REFORM EXISTING WEALTH TAXES FIRST (council tax, CGT, IHT). 
Annual wealth tax as a backstop.** **Confidence: MEDIUM.**

### Assumption 4: Should CGT be equalised with income tax?

**STRONG ECONOMIC CASE. REVENUE SIGNIFICANT.**

Current CGT rates (10-20%) are well below income tax rates (20-45%). 
This creates incentives to reclassify income as capital gains 
(particularly for business owners, private equity). The IFS has 
long argued for equalisation as the most economically efficient 
reform. Labour raised CGT in October 2024 but not to income tax 
parity.

Revenue estimate: full equalisation could raise £10-15bn/yr (IFS/OBR 
estimates vary due to behavioural response uncertainty).

Risk: May discourage genuine risk-taking investment. Counter: can 
exempt genuine entrepreneurial gains through targeted relief 
(Business Asset Disposal Relief) while closing the income-reclassification 
loophole.

**Verdict: ECONOMICALLY JUSTIFIED.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Is council tax reform possible?

**OVERDUE. CURRENT SYSTEM IS INDEFENSIBLE.**

Council tax bands are based on 1991 property values. A £10m London 
townhouse pays roughly 4x the council tax of a £100,000 terraced house 
— a ratio of 4:1 on a 100:1 value difference. 56% of the public 
support proportional council tax reform (YouGov).

The IFS recommends replacing council tax with a proportional property 
tax (1% of value, with protections for cash-poor asset-rich 
pensioners). This would be revenue-neutral in aggregate but would 
shift burden from lower-value to higher-value properties. Politically 
toxic because losers (homeowners in expensive areas) are more vocal 
than winners.

**Verdict: REFORM IS OVERDUE AND JUSTIFIED. Every serious tax 
analyst recommends it.** **Confidence: HIGH on economics. LOW on 
political feasibility.**

### Assumption 6: Can the UK fund its public services without raising the big three taxes?

**NOT SUSTAINABLY.**

IT + NI + VAT = two-thirds of revenue. The IFS shows that raising tens 
of billions from other sources while keeping these frozen is "difficult 
but not impossible." Options: CGT equalisation (~£10-15bn), council tax 
reform (~£5-10bn revenue-neutral rebalance), wealth-related tax reform, 
environmental taxes (carbon tax, aviation duty). Combined, these might 
raise £20-30bn — significant but not enough to close the gap between 
current revenue and the spending needed for NHS (£20-25bn), social care 
(£5bn), housing (£5-10bn), and defence (rising).

The honest answer: funding the public services the evidence says are 
needed requires either raising the big three taxes, accepting lower 
services, or sustained borrowing. The manifesto tax locks make the 
first option politically impossible.

**Verdict: TAX LOCKS ARE A MISTAKE.** They prioritise electoral 
convenience over fiscal honesty. **Confidence: HIGH** (IFS, OBR, 
every serious fiscal institution).

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The policy:

Honest taxation that funds public services at the level the evidence 
says is needed, through progressive reform of the existing system 
rather than new headline taxes — with the long-term goal of a simpler, 
more coherent tax system.

### The mechanism:

**Immediate (Year 1-2):**
- Equalise CGT with income tax rates (with targeted Business Asset 
  Disposal Relief for genuine entrepreneurs). Revenue: ~£10-15bn/yr
- End threshold freeze and index thresholds to inflation from 2027/28 
  (honest about the stealth tax)
- Increase HMRC funding by £1bn to improve tax gap collection 
  (IFS: £1 invested in HMRC returns ~£5-18 in revenue)
- Maintain corporation tax at 25% with full expensing (stability)

**Medium-term (Year 2-4):**
- Council tax reform: replace bands with proportional property tax at 
  ~0.5-1% of current value, with deferral mechanism for asset-rich 
  cash-poor households. Revenue-neutral in aggregate but progressive 
  in distribution.
- Reform IHT: close the agricultural/business property relief loopholes 
  that allow the wealthiest estates to pay near-zero IHT. Revenue: 
  ~£1-3bn/yr
- Environmental tax reform: aviation duty restructured, carbon border 
  adjustment (cross-domain → Energy P16), road pricing pilot

**Long-term (Year 4+):**
- If public services still underfunded after above reforms: be honest 
  about the need to raise IT, NI, or VAT. The manifesto states 
  assumptions and will update position based on evidence of whether 
  other reforms generate sufficient revenue
- Consider hypothecated NHS/social care levy (politically easier 
  than general tax rise, economically equivalent)
- Annual wealth tax as backstop only if wealth-related tax reform 
  insufficient (evidence suggests reform is better first step)

**What we won't do:**
- No unfunded tax cuts (Truss lesson)
- No tax lock pledges (they constrain good governance)
- No pretending the tax gap will magically fund everything
- No cutting spending on public services that are already failing

### The assumptions:

1. CGT equalisation raises ~£10-15bn (behavioural response uncertain)
2. Council tax reform is politically deliverable (historically toxic)
3. HMRC investment returns £5-18 per £1 spent (evidence strong)
4. IHT reform doesn't trigger agricultural land sales at scale
5. Growth is not sufficient on its own to close the fiscal gap
6. Public will accept honest communication about the trilemma
7. Environmental taxes can be designed progressively (protecting 
   low-income households)
8. The fiscal position doesn't deteriorate further (recession risk)

### International precedent:

- **Denmark/Sweden:** Tax at 45-50% of GDP (UK ~37%). Fund universal 
  public services. Higher satisfaction with services. Proves that 
  high-tax, high-service model works — but requires social consent.
- **Germany:** Council-tax equivalent (Grundsteuer) reformed 2025 
  after constitutional court ruling. Proves reform is possible when 
  courts force it.
- **Japan:** Consumption tax (VAT equivalent) raised gradually from 
  3% to 10% over decades. Proves gradual increase is survivable.
- **Norway:** Annual wealth tax at low rate (~1%). Some capital 
  flight but revenue maintained. Cautionary on high-rate wealth tax.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Revenue from proposed reforms (estimated):**
- CGT equalisation: £10-15bn/yr
- HMRC investment (£1bn → £5-18bn return): £4-17bn/yr
- IHT reform: £1-3bn/yr
- Council tax reform: revenue-neutral (redistribution, not raising)
- Environmental taxes: £2-5bn/yr
- Total new revenue: ~£17-40bn/yr (wide range due to uncertainty)

**What this funds (cross-domain):**
- NHS gap: £20-25bn/yr (→ NHS domain)
- Social care: £5bn/yr (→ NHS domain)
- Housing investment: £5-10bn/yr (→ Housing domain)
- Energy transition: contribution to £40bn/yr (→ Energy domain)
- Education, welfare, defence: TBD

**Sensitivity:**
- If CGT behavioural response is larger than expected: revenue 
  lower, need to consider IT/NI/VAT
- If council tax reform blocked: lose redistribution, not revenue
- If recession hits: tax revenue falls across all measures, 
  borrowing required as countercyclical buffer

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. Tax reform may trigger capital flight.** CGT equalisation and 
wealth-related reforms could encourage the wealthy to relocate. 
Norway has experienced some of this. The UK's advantages (language, 
legal system, timezone, culture) provide some protection, but the 
risk is real and should be monitored.

**2. Council tax reform may be genuinely impossible.** Every government 
since 1991 has avoided revaluation. The political cost (millions of 
homeowners seeing higher bills) may exceed any government's tolerance. 
If so, the evidence-based position is correct but undeliverable — a 
recurring theme across this manifesto.

**3. The growth argument has some merit.** While tax cuts don't pay 
for themselves, targeted reforms (planning, investment incentives, 
trade deals) can increase growth and therefore revenue. The position 
above may understate the growth dividend from other domain reforms 
(especially housing/planning).

**4. Hypothecated taxes may be better politics.** A visible "NHS levy" 
may be more acceptable to the public than general tax rises, even 
though economists correctly note hypothecation is economically 
meaningless. Sometimes good politics matters more than good economics 
for getting the right outcome.

**5. Public may genuinely prefer lower taxes and lower services.** The 
evidence-based position assumes the public wants the NHS, housing, 
and social care funding this manifesto proposes. If the public would 
actually prefer American-style low-tax-low-service, the entire fiscal 
framework needs rethinking. Polling suggests otherwise (75% support 
wealth tax, consistent NHS priority) but revealed preferences may 
differ from stated ones.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. Scrap Tax Locks
- **What:** No party should pledge not to raise specific taxes. 
  Instead, pledge fiscal rules (debt falling as % GDP, current 
  budget balanced) and let tax decisions follow from spending needs.
- **Evidence:** IFS calls tax locks "a mistake." They constrain 
  good governance and force dishonesty.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Every fiscal institution agrees.

### 2. Land Value Tax (Pilot)
- **What:** Tax the unimproved value of land. Economically efficient 
  because land supply is fixed — a tax on land doesn't reduce supply 
  (unlike taxes on income or consumption). Pilot in one region.
- **Evidence:** Adam Smith, Milton Friedman, and the IFS all endorse 
  LVT in principle. Operationally complex but theoretically optimal. 
  Could replace council tax, stamp duty, and business rates in a 
  unified land tax. Denmark, Estonia, and parts of Australia/US 
  use variants.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on economics. LOW on political deliverability.

### 3. Unified Income Tax + NI
- **What:** Merge Income Tax and National Insurance into a single, 
  transparent income tax. Currently NI creates complexity, confusion, 
  and different treatment of earned vs unearned income.
- **Evidence:** IFS, OBR, and multiple think tanks have recommended 
  merger for decades. The Conservatives pledged to work toward it 
  but never delivered. Simplification reduces compliance costs and 
  makes the tax system more honest.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on merit. MODERATE on delivery.

### 4. Carbon Tax with Dividend
- **What:** Direct carbon tax replacing the patchwork of green 
  levies, ETS, and climate policies. Revenue recycled as equal 
  per-capita dividend to every household. Progressive: low-carbon 
  households gain, high-carbon households pay.
- **Evidence:** Economists across the spectrum endorse carbon pricing 
  as the most efficient decarbonisation tool. Canada implemented a 
  carbon tax with dividend (currently under political pressure but 
  economically effective). Cross-domain → Energy.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on economics. MEDIUM on politics.

### 5. HMRC Transformation
- **What:** Double HMRC's compliance budget over 5 years. Every £1 
  invested returns £5-18. This is the highest-ROI "spending" in 
  government. Additionally: pre-populated tax returns (as in 
  Scandinavia), real-time tax calculation, simplified self-assessment.
- **Evidence:** HMRC's own data shows returns on compliance 
  investment. Nordic countries demonstrate pre-populated returns 
  reduce errors and increase revenue.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.
