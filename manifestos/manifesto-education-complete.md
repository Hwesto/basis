# EDUCATION & SKILLS
## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

---

## The Problem

UK education has been degraded by a decade of funding cuts masked by 
structural reorganisation. Three measurable dimensions:

**Funding has been cut across every phase.** School spending per pupil 
fell 9% in real terms between 2009/10 and 2019/20 (IFS). College 
funding per student fell 14% in the same period — sixth form funding 
fell 28%. University tuition fees have been frozen in nominal terms 
since 2017, losing ~30% of real value to inflation. SEND costs are 
soaring with no funded solution. The Education Policy Institute 
concludes that no major party's manifesto commits to adequate funding.

**The disadvantage gap is widening.** The attainment gap between 
disadvantaged pupils and their peers has widened since the pandemic 
and was already stagnating before it. The EPI identifies this as the 
central challenge no party adequately addresses. Child poverty — which 
directly drives educational underperformance — is rising, with the 
two-child benefit limit affecting an additional 670,000 children by 
2029 (IFS).

**The skills system is broken and constantly reorganised.** The IFS 
notes that "few areas of public policy have experienced as much change 
as skills policy." Successive governments have restructured 
qualifications, funding mechanisms, and institutional frameworks 
without resolving the fundamental problem: the UK underinvests in 
technical and vocational education relative to comparable countries. 
Apprenticeship starts fell after the levy was introduced. The 
construction, care, and energy sectors face acute skills shortages 
that directly constrain delivery in other domains.

The honest framing: education funding was cut more than almost any 
other public service during austerity, and no party has committed to 
restoring it. The UK spends less per student on post-16 education than 
almost any OECD comparator. This is the pipeline that produces the 
NHS workforce, the construction workforce, the energy transition 
workforce, and the social care workforce. Every other domain's 
workforce plan depends on an education system that is underfunded.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** Protect day-to-day school spending per pupil in real 
terms. Rebuild 500 schools. Expand academies. Close "low quality" 
university courses to fund 100,000 apprenticeships. National Service 
for school leavers. Ban mobile phones in schools (statutory). Lift 
faith school cap. Restrict international student dependants.

**Labour:** Recruit 6,500 new teachers in shortage subjects. Reform 
Ofsted — end single-word judgements. Broaden curriculum (standing 
commission). Growth and Skills Levy (broaden apprenticeship levy). 
3,000 nurseries in schools. Skills England. Child poverty strategy. 
Young Futures Hubs for mental health. VAT on private schools to fund 
state sector. 10-year R&D budgets for universities. No commitment on 
core school funding levels.

**Liberal Democrats:** Increase school funding per pupil above inflation 
annually. Increase college funding above inflation. Reform School 
Teachers' Review Body for independent pay recommendations. Fund all 
teacher trainee posts. £5,000 Lifelong Skills Grant for all adults. 
Reintroduce maintenance grants. Invest 3% GDP in R&D by 2030. 
Rejoin Erasmus.

**Green:** Fully fund all HE students — restore grants, scrap tuition 
fees, cancel graduate debt. Restore public health budgets in schools. 
Abolish Ofsted. End formal assessments. £30bn+ R&D over 5 years. 
150,000 social homes/year includes school places. Free school meals 
universal.

**Reform UK:** Scrap interest on student loans. Restrict undergraduate 
numbers. Enforce minimum entry standards. Cut funding to universities 
undermining free speech. "Patriotic curriculum." Ban teaching of 
"woke and transgender ideology."

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Is school funding adequate?

**NO. CUTS HAVE BEEN REAL AND DAMAGING.**

IFS: 9% real-terms cut per pupil 2009-2020. The "recovery" since 
2020 has roughly restored funding to 2010 levels — but costs have 
risen (SEND, energy, employer NI). Neither Conservative nor Labour 
committed to real-terms increases above inflation. The Lib Dems' 
commitment (above inflation annually) is the minimum to prevent 
further erosion.

The SEND funding crisis is the largest unfunded pressure: local 
authority SEND deficits are now billions. The government has announced 
compensation for 90% of historic SEND deficits, but the underlying 
cost drivers remain unaddressed.

**Verdict: FUNDING IS INSUFFICIENT.** At minimum, above-inflation 
increases needed with SEND reform. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Does closing "low quality" courses fund apprenticeships?

**LOGICALLY FLAWED.**

The Conservative proposal assumes students on closed courses don't 
simply enrol elsewhere on different loans. The IFS notes this only 
saves money "if these students don't just go somewhere else." 
University funding is already in crisis — closing courses without 
addressing the tuition fee freeze pushes institutions toward 
insolvency.

**Verdict: NOT CREDIBLE as a funding mechanism.** 
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 3: Will the Growth and Skills Levy improve skills provision?

**DIRECTION CORRECT. DETAIL INSUFFICIENT.**

Labour's broadening of the apprenticeship levy addresses a real 
problem: the narrow levy was driving down apprenticeship quality 
and starts. Allowing employers to fund a wider range of training 
is evidence-aligned. But the IFS notes "big questions over design" 
remain — what qualifies as a "training unit" is undefined.

Cross-domain: this is the mechanism that LAB-I06 (immigration skills 
linkage) depends on. If the Growth and Skills Levy doesn't produce 
domestic workers, immigration dependency persists.

**Verdict: RIGHT DIRECTION, insufficient detail.** 
**Confidence: MEDIUM.**

### Assumption 4: Should tuition fees be scrapped?

**REGRESSIVE UNLESS REPLACEMENT FUNDING IS SECURED.**

The Green proposal (scrap fees, cancel debt) costs ~£15-20bn/year. 
The IFS has consistently shown that graduate contributions through 
income-contingent loans are progressive — high earners repay more, 
low earners are forgiven. Scrapping fees and funding through general 
taxation would transfer costs from graduates (who benefit) to 
non-graduates (who don't).

However: the current system is broken. Fees frozen since 2017 have 
lost ~30% real value. Universities face a funding crisis. The system 
satisfies nobody — students feel exploited, universities are 
underfunded, taxpayers subsidise unpaid loans.

**Verdict: REFORM NEEDED but fee abolition is regressive. 
Better: restore real value of fees + expand maintenance grants for 
disadvantaged students.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Does early years investment have the highest ROI?

**YES. STRONGEST EVIDENCE IN EDUCATION.**

The Nobel laureate James Heckman's research shows that returns on 
investment in education decline with age — early years interventions 
yield 7-10% annual returns. Sure Start (Labour 1998-2010) showed 
measurable improvements in child health and development. The IFS 
confirms a shift in spending toward younger children produces better 
outcomes per pound.

All parties propose expanding free early years. Labour's 3,000 
nurseries in schools is the most specific delivery mechanism. But 
quality is as important as quantity — expanding places without 
investing in early years workforce (low pay, high turnover) 
undermines outcomes.

**Verdict: EARLY YEARS IS HIGHEST ROI.** Invest in quality and 
workforce, not just places. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 6: Is Ofsted reform needed?

**YES. BUT ABOLITION IS NOT EVIDENCE-SUPPORTED.**

The EPI notes that Labour's reform (end single-word judgements) is 
"welcome." The coroner's inquest into headteacher Ruth Perry's death 
(2023) highlighted the damage Ofsted's approach can cause. However, 
the EPI also concludes that the Green proposal to abolish Ofsted 
"is not supported by research evidence and may lead to falling 
standards and widening attainment gaps."

Accountability is necessary. The form it takes needs reform.

**Verdict: REFORM, NOT ABOLISH.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 7: Can the teacher workforce crisis be solved?

**NOT WITHOUT PAY AND CONDITIONS REFORM.**

Teacher vacancies are at record levels. Recruitment targets missed 
in most subjects for most years. Retention is the bigger problem — 
teachers leave due to workload, pay erosion, and accountability 
pressure. The Lib Dem proposal (independent pay review body) is the 
most structurally sound — removes political interference from pay 
decisions.

Cross-domain: 1 in 5 NHS staff are foreign nationals partly because 
domestic training was underfunded for decades. Education is repeating 
the same pattern — underinvest in the workforce, then depend on 
immigration to fill gaps.

**Verdict: PAY AND CONDITIONS ARE THE BINDING CONSTRAINT.** 
Recruitment pledges without retention reform are insufficient.
**Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The mechanism:

**Schools:**
- Above-inflation funding per pupil annually (Lib Dem standard minimum)
- SEND reform: national funding body, reduce local authority deficit burden
- Ofsted reform: end single-word judgements, shift to improvement-focused 
  inspection
- Teacher pay: independent review body (Lib Dem model), fully fund 
  recommended rises
- Disadvantage: triple the Pupil Premium over 10 years, targeted at 
  evidence-based interventions

**Post-16:**
- Align 16-18 funding with secondary baseline (Labour commitment — hold 
  them to it)
- Growth and Skills Levy: broaden but with clear quality standards
- Restore college funding to 2010 levels in real terms over parliament
- T-Levels and apprenticeships as genuine parity-of-esteem route

**Higher Education:**
- Increase tuition fee cap with inflation (restore real value)
- Expand maintenance grants for disadvantaged students
- 10-year R&D budgets (Labour — excellent proposal)
- Rejoin Erasmus or equivalent (Lib Dem)

**Early Years:**
- Nurseries in schools (Labour — good delivery mechanism)
- Early years workforce pay parity with school teaching assistants
- Sure Start 2.0: restore children's centres with evidence-based 
  programming
- Universal free school meals (Green — evidence supports for primary)

**Skills for other domains:**
- Energy: retrofit training pipeline (→ Energy P07, Housing P07)
- NHS: double medical school places (→ NHS LTWP)
- Construction: construction skills fund tied to housing targets
- Social care: career pathway with funded training
- Digital: AI/data skills embedded across post-16

### The assumptions:

1. Above-inflation funding is fiscally sustainable (→ Taxation)
2. Teacher pay reform attracts and retains enough staff
3. Growth and Skills Levy produces quality outcomes
4. Early years workforce can be expanded at quality
5. SEND reform reduces costs without reducing support
6. University funding model survives another parliament
7. Skills pipeline delivers workers fast enough for other domain needs
8. Disadvantage interventions close the gap over 10-15 years

### International precedent:

- **Finland:** Teacher profession high-status, highly selective (10:1 
  applicants), well-paid. Consistently top PISA performer. Proves that 
  investing in teacher quality produces results.
- **Germany:** Dual vocational system (apprenticeships integrated with 
  classroom learning). Youth unemployment among lowest in Europe. Proves 
  parity of esteem for technical education is achievable.
- **South Korea/Singapore:** Heavy investment in early years and teacher 
  training. Rapid improvement in PISA scores over 20 years. Proves 
  that education investment produces measurable returns at national level.
- **Denmark:** Free university education funded through taxation. High 
  participation, low dropout. But: requires high-tax social contract.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- Above-inflation school funding: ~£2-3bn/yr additional
- SEND reform: £1-2bn/yr (restructuring)
- College funding restoration: ~£1bn/yr
- Early years expansion + quality: ~£1-2bn/yr
- Teacher pay reform: ~£1-2bn/yr
- Maintenance grants: ~£1-2bn/yr
- Total: ~£7-12bn/yr additional

**Hard benefits:**
- Heckman: early years returns 7-10% annually
- Each additional year of education → ~8-13% higher lifetime earnings 
  (Mincer equation, widely replicated)
- Reduced NEET rates → less welfare spending, more tax revenue
- Skills pipeline → reduced immigration dependency (→ Immigration)
- Construction skills → housing delivery (→ Housing)
- Health workforce → NHS delivery (→ NHS)

**Sensitivity:**
- If funding only maintains real terms (not increases): slow 
  deterioration continues but crisis avoided
- If teacher recruitment doesn't improve: quality falls despite 
  funding — pay is necessary but not sufficient

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. Money may not be the binding constraint.** Some evidence suggests 
that how money is spent matters more than how much. Targeted 
interventions (tutoring, early reading programmes) may produce better 
outcomes than general funding increases. The Education Endowment 
Foundation's evidence toolkit supports this view.

**2. The skills system may need more radical reform.** Broadening 
the apprenticeship levy may not be enough. The German dual system 
requires employer commitment and institutional infrastructure the 
UK doesn't have. Building it takes decades, not one parliament.

**3. University fee reform may trigger institutional collapse.** 
Many universities are financially precarious. Any change to the 
fee regime (increase or abolition) creates winners and losers 
among institutions. Some may close — which could be the right 
outcome for over-provision but would be politically difficult.

**4. The "parity of esteem" problem may be cultural, not policy.** 
British culture values academic over vocational education. No 
amount of funding or branding has changed this in 50 years. 
Germany's success is cultural as much as structural.

**5. AI may transform education faster than policy can adapt.** 
Personalised AI tutoring, automated assessment, and adaptive 
learning could make current debates about class sizes, teacher 
numbers, and institutional structures less relevant within a decade.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. Education Endowment Foundation at Scale
- **What:** Make EEF's evidence-based intervention toolkit mandatory 
  for Pupil Premium spending. Currently schools can spend PP on 
  anything. Requiring evidence-based approaches would dramatically 
  improve outcomes for disadvantaged pupils.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. EEF evidence base is strong.

### 2. National Retrofit Training Academy
- **What:** Dedicated training institution for insulation, heat pump 
  installation, and building retrofit skills. Cross-domain with Energy 
  and Housing. The workforce for the National Insulation Service doesn't 
  exist — this creates it.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Skills gap is the binding constraint on 
  retrofit delivery.

### 3. Lifelong Learning Entitlement (Done Properly)
- **What:** The Lib Dems' £5,000 Lifelong Skills Grant is the right 
  concept. Make it real: portable, employer-matchable, usable at any 
  accredited provider. Focus on mid-career transitions (→ Energy 
  workforce transition, social care career pathway).
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Concept proven in Singapore (SkillsFuture).

### 4. AI Tutoring as Public Service
- **What:** Commission a free, evidence-based AI tutoring platform 
  for every school pupil. Personalised learning, homework support, 
  exam preparation. Not replacing teachers — supplementing them, 
  especially in shortage subjects.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Technology exists. Quality assurance and 
  safeguarding need careful design.

### 5. Integrated Children's Services
- **What:** Merge education, health visiting, early years, and 
  children's social care into a single local service. The current 
  fragmentation means vulnerable children fall through gaps between 
  services. Sure Start demonstrated what integrated delivery looks 
  like — rebuild it with modern evidence.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on principle. MODERATE on delivery.
