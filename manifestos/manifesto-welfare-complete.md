# WELFARE & POVERTY
## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

---

## The Problem

The UK welfare system fails the people it exists to protect while 
costing more than ever. Three measurable dimensions:

**Child poverty is rising and policy-driven.** Over 4 million children 
live in low-income families. The two-child benefit limit — introduced 
in 2015 — affects 483,000 families and 1.67 million children. The DWP 
estimates 300,000 children are currently in relative poverty directly 
as a result of this single policy — equivalent to 100 children pushed 
into poverty every day since introduction. Children growing up in 
poverty earn approximately 25% less at age 30 than their peers.

**Economic inactivity is the hidden crisis.** 2.8 million people are 
economically inactive due to long-term sickness — up 800,000 since 
pre-pandemic (cross-domain → NHS). This costs the economy tens of 
billions in lost output and increased benefit spending. The NHS 
waiting list (7.6 million) is a direct driver: people can't work 
while waiting for treatment. The IPPR estimates clearing the NHS 
backlog would generate £73bn in economic benefit.

**The welfare system punishes rather than supports.** Universal Credit's 
sanctions regime, the benefit cap, the bedroom tax, and the two-child 
limit collectively create a system designed to minimise spending rather 
than minimise poverty. The IFS notes that UC's "no change to spending 
on social security benefits" (Labour manifesto) coexists with rising 
poverty — the system is failing by its own metrics. Meanwhile, the 
welfare budget is rising because of disability and housing costs that 
the system can't control.

The honest framing: the welfare budget is rising not because of 
generosity but because of failure elsewhere. Housing costs drive 
housing benefit (→ Housing). NHS waiting lists drive sickness benefits 
(→ NHS). Low wages drive in-work poverty (→ Education/Skills). 
Welfare spending is a symptom of underinvestment in every other domain.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** Maintain two-child limit. £12bn welfare savings. 
Tighten disability benefits (reform Work Capability Assessment). Focus 
on getting people into work. Triple lock on state pension maintained. 
Reduce welfare rolls through sanctions and conditionality.

**Labour:** Initially maintained two-child limit, then announced 
removal in November 2025 Budget (Bill introduced January 2026, 
effective April 2026). Child poverty strategy. UC review. Get Britain 
Working plan — tackle NHS waits, mental health, Access to Work. 
Fair Pay Agreement for social care. Triple lock maintained. Reform 
Work Capability Assessment.

**Liberal Democrats:** Remove two-child limit and benefit cap. End deep 
poverty within a decade. Independent commission on UC adequacy. Carer's 
minimum wage (+£2/hr). Expand Carer's Allowance eligibility. Regular 
respite breaks for unpaid carers. Caring as protected characteristic.

**Green:** Universal Basic Income pilot. Remove all benefit caps and 
limits. Restore benefits to pre-austerity levels. End sanctions regime. 
Free personal care (£20bn). Wealth taxes to fund welfare expansion.

**Reform UK:** £50bn spending cuts across government. Focus on work. 
Tax cuts to incentivise employment. Cut overseas aid to fund domestic 
welfare (partially). Prioritise British citizens for social housing 
and benefits.

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Does the two-child limit reduce poverty or incentivise work?

**IT INCREASES POVERTY. WORK INCENTIVE EFFECT IS SMALL.**

The DWP's own modelling: removing the limit would lift 450,000 
children out of relative poverty by end of parliament. 2 million 
children would see household income increase. The IFS: affected 
households lose £4,300/yr on average, representing 10% of income.

On work incentives: a 2025 study found the limit increased 
employment of affected mothers by ~3 percentage points (from ~50% 
baseline). This is "meaningful" but means the "substantial majority 
of mothers do not respond through employment and hence will have a 
lower income." The policy trades poverty for a marginal work 
incentive effect. Cost of retention: £2.4bn/yr rising to £3.2bn/yr.

Labour reversed the policy in November 2025 after initially 
maintaining it. The Bill is in parliament.

**Verdict: REMOVAL IS CORRECT.** The poverty effect vastly exceeds 
the work incentive effect. Government's own analysis confirms this.
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Can welfare savings of £12bn be achieved?

**THE IFS SAYS THIS "LOOKS DIFFICULT IN THE EXTREME."**

The Conservative pledge to fund NI cuts through £12bn welfare savings 
had no credible delivery mechanism. Disability benefits are rising due 
to genuine need (long-term sickness up 800,000). Sanctions increase 
destitution without reliably increasing employment. Tightening 
eligibility shifts costs to other services (NHS, homelessness, 
criminal justice) rather than saving them.

**Verdict: NOT CREDIBLE.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 3: Is Universal Basic Income feasible?

**THEORETICALLY ATTRACTIVE. PRACTICALLY CHALLENGING AT UK SCALE.**

Finland's UBI pilot (2017-2018): modest positive effects on wellbeing 
and trust but no significant employment effect. The pilot was small 
(2,000 people) and short (2 years). No full-scale UBI has been 
implemented in a comparable economy. Cost at meaningful level 
(e.g., £10,000/year for all UK adults): ~£500bn/year — roughly 
equal to total current government spending.

A partial UBI (guaranteed minimum income below UC level) is more 
feasible but functionally similar to UC with less conditionality.

**Verdict: PILOT WORTH EXPLORING. Full implementation not fiscal 
reality.** **Confidence: MEDIUM.**

### Assumption 4: Does "getting people into work" solve poverty?

**NOT WHEN WORK DOESN'T PAY.**

60% of children in poverty live in working households. In-work 
poverty is the dominant form of poverty in the UK. Low wages, 
insecure contracts (zero-hours), high housing costs, and 
inadequate childcare mean that employment alone doesn't prevent 
poverty. The UC taper rate means some workers face effective 
marginal tax rates above 60%.

The honest answer: work is necessary but not sufficient. The 
welfare system must address in-work poverty, not just worklessness.

**Verdict: WORK ALONE IS INSUFFICIENT.** Pay, housing costs, and 
childcare are the co-constraints. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Is the disability benefit rise driven by fraud or genuine need?

**GENUINE NEED. DRIVEN BY LONG-TERM SICKNESS RISE.**

The 800,000 increase in economically inactive people since the 
pandemic is driven by mental health conditions, musculoskeletal 
problems, and long Covid — not fraud. The NHS waiting list prevents 
treatment that would enable return to work. The DWP's own data 
shows fraud in disability benefits is a small fraction of total 
spending. Tightening eligibility without addressing the health 
causes shifts costs, it doesn't save them.

**Verdict: ADDRESS THE HEALTH CAUSES, NOT THE BENEFIT ELIGIBILITY.**
**Confidence: HIGH.** (Cross-domain → NHS)

### Assumption 6: Does the benefit cap reduce poverty?

**NO. IT SHIFTS IT.**

The benefit cap limits total household benefit income regardless of 
need. In high-cost areas (London, South East), the cap forces 
families into cheaper housing, disrupting children's education, 
employment, and support networks. The cap doesn't reduce housing 
costs — it reduces families' ability to afford housing. The 
underlying problem is housing cost (→ Housing), not benefit 
generosity.

**Verdict: THE BENEFIT CAP ADDRESSES A SYMPTOM, NOT A CAUSE.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The mechanism:

**Immediate:**
- Complete removal of two-child limit (in progress — hold Labour 
  to April 2026 implementation)
- Review benefit cap with view to regional variation (London cap 
  is punitive given housing costs)
- Right to work for asylum seekers after 3 months (cross-domain 
  → Immigration)
- Restore UC adequacy: independent commission to recommend annual 
  uprates (Lib Dem model)

**Child poverty:**
- Child poverty strategy with binding targets (Labour committed 
  to strategy — hold them to targets)
- Universal free school meals in primary (Green — evidence supports)
- Sure Start 2.0 / integrated children's services (→ Education)
- Childcare reform: extend free hours, improve quality, pay workforce

**Economic inactivity:**
- Treat long-term sickness as joint NHS-DWP priority 
  (cross-domain → NHS innovation 5)
- Dedicated NHS capacity for return-to-work rehabilitation
- Access to Work backlog clearance
- Mental health provision at scale (→ NHS)
- UC reform: reduce effective marginal tax rate for 
  low-income workers

**Social care:**
- Fair Pay Agreement: minimum £12/hr for care workers
- Career structure with training pathway (→ Education)
- National Care Service (→ NHS)

**Pensions:**
- Maintain triple lock (all parties agree — rare consensus)
- But: acknowledge triple lock is fiscally unsustainable 
  long-term. The OBR projects pension spending growing faster 
  than revenue. Intergenerational fairness requires honest 
  conversation about pension costs vs working-age support.

### The assumptions:

1. Two-child limit removal is implemented (Bill in progress)
2. UC adequacy commission is independent and evidence-led
3. NHS backlog reduction enables return-to-work at scale
4. Social care fair pay attracts domestic workforce
5. Child poverty strategy includes binding targets with accountability
6. Childcare expansion doesn't sacrifice quality for quantity
7. Triple lock remains politically untouchable (all parties)
8. Public accepts that welfare spending rises because of system failures 
   elsewhere, not benefit generosity

### International precedent:

- **Denmark:** "Flexicurity" model — generous benefits + active labour 
  market policy + flexible hiring/firing. Low poverty, low long-term 
  unemployment. Requires high taxation and strong institutional capacity.
- **Finland UBI pilot:** Modest wellbeing gains, no employment effect. 
  Suggests UBI improves quality of life but doesn't solve employment.
- **Germany:** Bürgergeld (replaced Hartz IV, 2023) — higher rates, 
  less punitive sanctions. Early evidence mixed on employment effects.
- **Scotland:** Scottish Child Payment (£26.70/week per child). Has 
  demonstrably reduced child poverty relative to rest of UK. Proves 
  that direct cash transfers to families work.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- Two-child limit removal: £2.4bn/yr (2026/27), rising to £3.2bn by 2030
- UC adequacy increase: £3-5bn/yr (depending on commission recommendations)
- Childcare expansion: £2-4bn/yr
- Social care fair pay: £2-5bn/yr (cross-domain → NHS)
- Total: ~£10-17bn/yr additional

**Hard benefits:**
- 450,000 children lifted from poverty (DWP estimate from two-child removal)
- Each child lifted from poverty: reduced lifetime public spending 
  on health, criminal justice, welfare (estimated £40,000-100,000 per child)
- Return-to-work from long-term sickness: each person = ~£25,000/yr 
  economic output + reduced benefits
- Scottish Child Payment: proven poverty reduction at modest cost
- Universal free school meals: improved attendance and attainment 
  (evidence from pilots)

**Unmonetised:**
- Intergenerational equity: children's life chances
- Social cohesion: poverty drives crime, poor health, exclusion
- Moral position: richest nations shouldn't have 4m children in poverty

**Sensitivity:**
- If two-child removal doesn't reduce poverty as modelled: DWP's own 
  analysis is the most conservative estimate. Direction is certain.
- If UC adequacy increases reduce work incentives: taper rate reform 
  can mitigate. Danish flexicurity model shows generous benefits + 
  active support produces low unemployment.
- If childcare expansion doesn't improve outcomes: quality is key — 
  early years workforce investment (→ Education) must accompany expansion.

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. Welfare generosity may genuinely reduce work incentives.** The 
two-child limit did increase maternal employment by 3 percentage points. 
More generous UC may reduce incentives at the margin. The Danish model 
shows this can be managed with active labour market policy, but the 
UK doesn't have Denmark's institutional capacity.

**2. The fiscal cost may be prohibitive.** £10-17bn/yr is significant. 
If taxation reform (→ Taxation) doesn't generate sufficient revenue, 
welfare expansion competes with NHS, housing, education, and defence. 
Prioritisation is necessary.

**3. Child poverty may be more structural than cash transfers can fix.** 
Housing costs, educational inequality, health disparities, and 
intergenerational disadvantage are multi-causal. Cash alone helps but 
doesn't solve the system. The platform's cross-domain approach captures 
this — but voters and politicians want simple solutions.

**4. The triple lock may be the biggest fiscal risk.** Pension spending 
is growing faster than any other welfare category. The triple lock 
guarantees above-inflation rises for pensioners while working-age 
benefits are frozen or cut. This is an intergenerational transfer 
that no party will address. The graph shows this tension but the 
political consensus means it's unlikely to change.

**5. Sanctions may have some role.** The evidence is mixed, not 
uniformly negative. Some sanctions research shows short-term 
employment effects. The question is whether punitive conditionality 
or supportive conditionality (Denmark) produces better outcomes. 
Evidence favours supportive, but the UK system is designed around 
punitive.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. Guaranteed Minimum Income Floor
- **What:** No household falls below a defined income floor regardless 
  of circumstances. Different from UBI (which is universal) — this 
  is targeted at the lowest-income households. Functionally, this 
  means UC rates must cover essentials (food, energy, housing) as 
  independently assessed.
- **Evidence:** JRF Minimum Income Standard identifies the gap between 
  UC rates and actual cost of essentials. The gap is currently 
  ~£35-50/week for a single adult.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on principle. MEDIUM on fiscal feasibility.

### 2. Automatic Benefits Enrolment
- **What:** Reverse the default — instead of requiring people to claim 
  benefits they're entitled to, automatically enrol them using HMRC 
  and DWP data. Non-take-up of means-tested benefits is estimated 
  at £10-15bn/yr. People in poverty are not claiming what they're 
  entitled to because the system is too complex.
- **Evidence:** The Netherlands and Nordic countries use automatic 
  enrolment for some benefits. HMRC's real-time information already 
  has the data to pre-populate UC claims.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Technology exists. Administrative will is the 
  constraint.

### 3. Scottish Child Payment Model for England
- **What:** Direct cash payment per child for low-income families 
  (currently £26.70/week in Scotland). Simple, universal within 
  target group, proven to reduce child poverty.
- **Evidence:** Scottish Government evaluation shows measurable poverty 
  reduction. The mechanism is direct and transparent. The two-child 
  limit removal is essentially this principle applied within UC.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Proven model.

### 4. Carers' Recognition Package
- **What:** Carer's minimum wage (+£2/hr as Lib Dem propose), 
  guaranteed respite, recognition in pension calculations, caring 
  as protected characteristic. Unpaid carers save the state an 
  estimated £162bn/year — more than total NHS spending.
- **Evidence:** Carers UK estimates unpaid care value at £162bn/yr. 
  Carers have worse health outcomes, lower lifetime earnings, and 
  higher poverty rates than non-carers.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. The economic case is overwhelming.

### 5. Integrated Welfare-Health-Housing Dashboard
- **What:** For each local authority, publish a real-time dashboard 
  showing: benefit spending, NHS waiting times, housing costs, 
  employment rates, and child poverty. Make the cross-domain 
  connections visible at local level. This is the platform concept 
  applied to welfare governance.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Technology straightforward. Political will 
  for transparency uncertain.
