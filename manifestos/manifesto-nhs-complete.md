# NHS & SOCIAL CARE
## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

---

## The Problem

The Darzi Report (September 2024) provides the definitive diagnosis. 
The NHS is in "critical condition" with "strong vital signs." Three 
measurable failures:

**Access has collapsed.** 7.6 million people waiting for treatment 
(June 2024) — up from 2.4 million in March 2010. Over 300,000 waiting 
more than a year. A&E 4-hour target hit 96.6% in 2011; by 2024 it was 
74.5%. Around 1 million people waiting for mental health services. GPs 
per 100,000 population declining 1.9% per year since 2016. The 18-week 
treatment target has not been met since 2016.

**Productivity has stalled while spending increased.** NHS productivity 
is at least 11.4% lower than 2019. From 2019 onwards, the previous 
government oversaw a 17% increase in hospital staff — but outcomes did 
not improve. Spending has shifted towards hospitals: hospital nursing up 
35% (2009-2023) while community nursing fell 5%. The NHS has got bigger 
without getting better.

**Social care is in crisis, and it's dragging the NHS down with it.** 
13% of hospital beds are occupied by patients waiting for social care — 
"bed blocking" that costs the NHS billions and delays treatment for 
everyone. Darzi describes social care as "dire" — under-resourced, 
under-valued, with a growing gap between those in need and those 
receiving publicly funded care. The workforce is collapsing: 19% non-UK 
nationals, and the overseas recruitment route has been closed.

**The root causes (Darzi):**
1. A decade of constrained funding (£37bn capital shortfall since 2010)
2. The pandemic amplified existing weaknesses
3. Lack of patient voice and staff engagement
4. Constant reorganisation of NHS structures
5. Worsening public health (more years in ill health, driven by 
   housing, income, employment — cross-domain)
6. Social care failure blocking hospital flow

The honest framing: the NHS is a system designed in 1948 running on 
infrastructure partly predating 1948 (20% of primary care estate), 
with a workforce plan that depends on overseas recruitment for 15 years, 
in a society that is getting sicker due to factors the NHS doesn't 
control. No amount of reorganisation fixes this without sustained 
multi-decade investment.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** Implement NHS Long Term Workforce Plan (LTWP) — 92,000 
more nurses, 28,000 more doctors by end of parliament. Reduce managers 
by 5,500. Extra £3.7bn/yr for social care. Cap care costs. New Hospitals 
Programme. AI and technology investment (£3.4bn). Waiting list focus.

**Labour:** 40,000 extra appointments/week (2% increase). Deliver LTWP. 
National Care Service. Fair Pay Agreement for social care. "Fit for 
the Future" fund for diagnostic technology. Reform Mental Health Act. 
NHS App as front door. Shift to prevention. 10-year plan (due 2025).

**Liberal Democrats:** £8.4bn/yr extra for health and social care by 
2028/29. 8,000 more GPs. Free personal care. Carer's minimum wage 
(+£2/hr). £1bn/yr extra for public health. Guarantee cancer treatment 
within 62 days. Royal College of Care Workers.

**Green:** £20bn free personal care. Restore public health budgets 
(+£1.5bn/yr). Repeal anti-union laws. Carer's minimum wage. Wellness 
service. Address social determinants. End distinction between health 
and social care.

**Reform UK:** £17bn/yr extra (by far the largest pledge — uncosted). 
Zero waiting lists in 2 years. Tax relief on private healthcare. 
"Fundamental reform" — no detail.

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Can waiting lists be cleared in one parliament?

**UNLIKELY WITHOUT MASSIVE ADDITIONAL FUNDING.**

Darzi: "unlikely that waiting lists can be cleared and other performance 
standards restored in one parliamentary term." IFS (Feb 2024): reducing 
to pre-pandemic levels "highly unlikely within the next parliament." 
Labour's 40,000 extra appointments = 2 million/year = 2% increase on 
145 million annual outpatient appointments. Reform's claim of zero 
waiting lists in 2 years has been "disputed by experts" (The Conversation).

The IPPR estimates clearing the backlog would generate £73bn in economic 
benefit (2023-2027) by returning people to work.

**Verdict: NOT ACHIEVABLE in 5 years without funding well beyond any 
manifesto commitment.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Is NHS funding sufficient?

**NO. EVERY MANIFESTO FALLS SHORT.**

Nuffield Trust: all three main parties' pledges would make the next 
four years the tightest in NHS history — tighter than coalition 
austerity. The £20-23bn gap between manifesto commitments and LTWP 
funding requirements would require productivity growth at 3-4x 
historic rates. King's Fund: "altogether more muted on NHS spending 
pledges than 2017 and 2019." Darzi identified a £37bn capital shortfall 
since 2010. NHS buildings maintenance backlog increased 188% over 
the past decade.

The Lib Dems' £8.4bn is the largest specific pledge from a main party 
but still only takes real-terms growth marginally above austerity levels 
(1.5% vs 1.4%).

**Verdict: SYSTEMIC UNDERFUNDING.** No manifesto closes the gap.
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 3: Can the workforce plan deliver?

**DEPENDS ON CONTINUED OVERSEAS RECRUITMENT FOR 15 YEARS.**

The LTWP targets doubling medical school places. This takes 10-15 years 
to produce trained doctors. In the interim, the NHS depends on overseas 
recruitment: 1 in 5 staff are foreign nationals, 27% of nurses, 50% 
of secondary-care doctors trained abroad (cross-domain → Immigration).

Labour has raised salary thresholds and closed the care worker overseas 
route (July 2025). Social care organisations describe this as risking 
"extreme workforce shortages." The RCN predicts nursing numbers will 
more than halve by 2029 without intervention.

**Verdict: WORKFORCE PLAN IS NECESSARY BUT INSUFFICIENT WITHOUT 
OVERSEAS RECRUITMENT IN THE INTERIM.** The immigration policy and 
NHS workforce policy are in direct tension.
**Confidence: HIGH.** (Cross-domain → Immigration A8, P07)

### Assumption 4: Will social care reform happen?

**EVERY GOVERNMENT HAS PROMISED AND FAILED FOR 25 YEARS.**

The Dilnot Commission (2011) recommended a cap on care costs. The 
Conservatives promised it in 2019, legislated for it, then delayed 
implementation twice. Labour has committed to a National Care Service 
but provided no roadmap or interim funding. The King's Fund notes that 
social care is the issue "parties prefer not to talk about." 13% of 
hospital beds blocked by social care patients is the single largest 
driver of A&E crisis.

Fair pay for social care (Labour's Fair Pay Agreement, Lib Dems' 
+£2/hr carer's minimum wage) is essential but costly: estimated 
£2-5bn/yr. Without it, domestic workers won't enter the sector and 
the overseas route is closing.

**Verdict: NO PARTY HAS A CREDIBLE SOCIAL CARE PLAN WITH ADEQUATE 
FUNDING.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Can technology and AI fix productivity?

**PARTIALLY. NOT A SUBSTITUTE FOR FUNDING.**

NHS productivity is 11.4% below 2019. The NHS is 15 years behind the 
private sector on technology (Darzi). Only 20% of NHS App users are 
active monthly despite 80% registration. Many NHS systems don't 
interoperate. 20% of primary care estate predates 1948.

AI in diagnostics (radiology, pathology) shows genuine promise — faster, 
more accurate, earlier cancer detection. But: technology deployed on 
crumbling infrastructure doesn't work. Digital transformation requires 
capital investment that hasn't happened.

**Verdict: TECHNOLOGY IS NECESSARY BUT REQUIRES CAPITAL INVESTMENT 
FIRST.** The £37bn shortfall must be addressed before AI can deliver.
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 6: Should the UK shift spending from hospitals to community/prevention?

**YES. EVERY SERIOUS ANALYSIS AGREES. NO GOVERNMENT HAS DONE IT.**

Darzi: "Since at least 2006, successive governments have promised to 
shift care away from hospitals and into the community." Community 
nursing down 5% while hospital nursing up 35%. Public health budgets 
were cut during austerity. Prevention spending is a fraction of 
treatment spending despite overwhelming evidence that prevention 
is more cost-effective.

The IPPR Darzi/Davies Commission (2024) argues for moving from a 
"sickness model" to a "health creation model" — addressing housing, 
income, employment as health interventions.

**Verdict: SHIFT TO PREVENTION IS THE SINGLE MOST COST-EFFECTIVE 
REFORM.** But it requires sustained investment over 10-20 years before 
savings materialise. Every government wants the savings but won't 
make the investment. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 7: Does private healthcare relieve NHS pressure?

**MIXED. MAY WORSEN WORKFORCE PROBLEMS.**

Reform UK proposes tax relief on private healthcare. The argument: 
people who go private reduce NHS demand. The counter-argument: the 
same doctors and nurses work in both systems. Tax relief subsidises 
private care for those who can afford it while drawing workforce away 
from the NHS. There is no evidence that expanding private healthcare 
reduces NHS waiting lists at system level — it may increase them by 
competing for staff.

**Verdict: NOT A SYSTEM-LEVEL SOLUTION.** May help individuals, 
harms the collective. **Confidence: MEDIUM.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The policy:

Sustained multi-decade investment in the NHS and social care as a 
single integrated system, with a genuine shift to prevention, 
community care, and technology — funded honestly, not through magical 
productivity assumptions.

### The mechanism:

**Funding:**
- Minimum 3.5% real-terms annual growth in NHS funding (historic 
  average) — not the 0.9-1.5% pledged in manifestos
- Clear the £37bn capital maintenance backlog over 10 years
- Ring-fenced prevention budget of £1bn+/yr (Lib Dem level)
- Social care funding: minimum £5bn/yr additional to fund Fair Pay 
  Agreement and close the care gap
- Honest about funding source: either taxation, borrowing, or both

**Workforce:**
- Deliver LTWP (double medical school places)
- Maintain overseas recruitment during 15-year domestic transition 
  (cross-domain → Immigration: do not close routes the NHS depends on)
- Social care fair pay: minimum £12/hr, career structure, training
- Address retention: flexible working, childcare, pension reform
- Stop the exodus: 15,000-23,000 doctors leaving annually (BMA)

**Social care integration:**
- National Care Service with guaranteed standards
- Free personal care for those with highest needs (Lib Dem/Green)
- Merge health and social care commissioning locally
- Unblock hospital beds: 13% occupied by social care patients
- Cap care costs (implement Dilnot — already legislated, twice delayed)

**Prevention shift:**
- Public health budgets restored to 2015/16 levels minimum
- Address social determinants: housing quality (cross-domain → Housing), 
  poverty (cross-domain → Welfare), employment security
- Preventive interventions: smoking cessation, obesity, alcohol, 
  mental health — all have evidence of cost-effectiveness
- Every £1 in prevention saves £4-14 in treatment (PHE estimates)

**Technology:**
- Capital investment FIRST, then digital transformation
- NHS App as genuine front door (not decorative)
- AI in diagnostics (radiology, pathology) — proven evidence base
- Interoperable digital records across all settings
- Virtual wards expansion (proven to reduce admissions)

### The assumptions:

1. 3.5% real-terms growth is politically sustainable
2. Social care fair pay attracts domestic workers
3. Prevention investment shows returns within 10-15 years
4. Overseas recruitment continues during domestic training scale-up
5. Technology adoption increases productivity (11.4% gap to close)
6. Capital investment is prioritised alongside revenue spending
7. Integration of health and social care can overcome institutional resistance
8. Public accepts honest conversation about NHS funding

### International precedent:

- **Denmark/Sweden/Norway:** ~10-11% GDP on health (UK ~10.3% but 
  with worse outcomes). Higher community care share. Integrated 
  social care. But: higher taxation.
- **Netherlands:** Mandatory social insurance model. Lower waiting 
  times, higher satisfaction. But: more expensive per capita.
- **Japan:** Oldest population in the world, universal coverage, 
  integrated long-term care insurance (2000). Proves that an aging 
  population can be served — but requires dedicated social care funding.
- **New Zealand:** Integrated health system reforms (2022). Early 
  evidence of improved coordination. Comparable NHS-style system.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- NHS funding gap to 3.5% growth: ~£10-15bn/yr above current plans
- Capital backlog clearance: £3.7bn/yr for 10 years
- Social care funding: £5bn+/yr additional
- Prevention: £1bn/yr ring-fenced
- Total: ~£20-25bn/yr above current spending

**Hard benefits:**
- IPPR: clearing backlog generates £73bn economic benefit (2023-2027)
- 2.8 million economically inactive due to long-term sickness — 
  returning even 10% to work = £5-10bn/yr in tax revenue
- 13% of hospital beds freed by social care reform = thousands of 
  additional elective procedures without building new capacity
- Prevention: £1 saves £4-14 (PHE)

**Unmonetised:**
- Life expectancy (UK falling behind peers)
- Quality of life (years in ill health increasing)
- Social contract (public satisfaction at all-time low)
- Economic productivity (healthier workers = higher output)

**Sensitivity:**
- If funding growth only 2.5% not 3.5%: slower improvement but 
  direction correct
- If social care reform fails again: hospital bed-blocking continues, 
  A&E crisis worsens, costs spiral
- If prevention returns take 20 years not 10: investment still correct 
  but political patience required

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. The funding ask may be impossible.** £20-25bn/yr additional is 
~1% of GDP. No party pledged this. The fiscal position may not support 
it. If so, the evidence-based position is correct but undeliverable.

**2. Prevention may not save money.** The intuition that prevention is 
cheaper than treatment is widely shared but not universally supported. 
Some preventive interventions extend life, increasing lifetime health 
costs. The evidence for cost-effectiveness is strong for specific 
interventions (smoking cessation, childhood vaccination) but weaker 
for population-wide programmes.

**3. Integration may be structurally impossible.** Every government 
since Blair has promised to integrate health and social care. All have 
failed. The institutional, financial, and cultural barriers between 
NHS and council-run social care may be more fundamental than any 
reform can overcome.

**4. The workforce plan may be too late.** Doubling medical school places 
takes 10-15 years. The workforce crisis is now. If overseas recruitment 
is restricted simultaneously, the gap widens before it closes.

**5. The NHS may need structural reform, not just funding.** Darzi 
identifies structural problems (constant reorganisation, lack of 
accountability, productivity stall despite staff increases) that money 
alone won't fix. The 10-year plan may need to be more radical than 
any party is willing to propose.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. Neighbourhood Health Service
- **What:** Shift 20% of hospital budgets to community-based 
  multidisciplinary teams over 10 years. Each team covers ~30,000 
  people: GPs, nurses, mental health, social care, physiotherapy 
  under one roof and one budget.
- **Evidence:** Darzi repeatedly identifies the hospital-community 
  imbalance. Buurtzorg (Netherlands) model demonstrates that 
  community nursing teams with autonomy deliver better outcomes at 
  lower cost. Costa Rica's EBAIS primary care teams achieve life 
  expectancy comparable to the US at 1/10th the cost.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on principle. MODERATE on UK delivery.

### 2. Social Prescribing at Scale
- **What:** GPs prescribe non-medical interventions: exercise, social 
  groups, nature, volunteering. NHS already doing this but at tiny 
  scale. Evidence base growing for effectiveness on loneliness, mild 
  depression, chronic pain management.
- **Evidence:** University of Westminster systematic review shows 
  benefits for wellbeing and reduced GP visits. NHSE has funded link 
  workers but scale is insufficient.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Evidence growing but long-term outcomes 
  data limited.

### 3. NHS as Anchor Institution
- **What:** Use NHS procurement, employment, and estate to drive 
  local economic development. The NHS is the largest employer in 
  most local economies — use that purchasing power to support local 
  food systems, green construction, and social enterprises.
- **Evidence:** Preston Model demonstrates anchor institution 
  approach works. Health Foundation's "NHS as anchor" programme has 
  published evidence of impact.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE-HIGH. Proven concept, needs scaling.

### 4. Integrated Health and Care Records
- **What:** Single patient record accessible across NHS, social care, 
  mental health, and community services. Currently fragmented across 
  dozens of systems. Estonia's e-health system is the model.
- **Evidence:** Fragmented records cause duplicate tests, medication 
  errors, and delays. NHS Digital estimates interoperability could save 
  £1bn+/yr. But UK government IT project record is poor.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on need. MODERATE on delivery.

### 5. Long-Term Sickness as Economic Emergency
- **What:** Treat the 2.8 million economically inactive due to 
  long-term sickness as an economic emergency requiring joint 
  DWP-NHS intervention. Dedicated NHS capacity for return-to-work 
  health assessments, musculoskeletal rehabilitation, and mental 
  health support.
- **Evidence:** IPPR and Darzi both identify this as a critical 
  economic issue. Each person returned to work generates ~£25,000/yr 
  in economic output plus reduced benefits.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. The economic case is overwhelming.

funding. The Nuffield Trust shows that Conservative and Labour plans 
would make the next parliament the tightest in NHS history. The Darzi
