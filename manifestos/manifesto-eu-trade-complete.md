## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft

## The Problem

Brexit has measurably damaged the UK economy and no party honestly 
states the scale.

**The GDP impact is now quantified.** The OBR estimates Brexit will 
reduce long-term UK GDP by 4%, with trade intensity declining 15%. 
A November 2025 NBER study (Bloom et al.) found Brexit has already 
reduced GDP by 6-8% — substantially worse than the OBR estimate. 
This is the single largest self-inflicted economic shock in modern 
UK history.

**Trade deals don't compensate.** The cumulative impact of all UK FTAs 
since Brexit — including CPTPP (0.06% GDP), Australia (0.08%), and 
hypothetical future deals with the US and India — totals less than 1% 
of GDP. The 4-8% lost from leaving the single market and customs union 
cannot be recovered by deals with more distant economies. The 
arithmetic is definitive.

**The "reset" is real but constrained.** Labour's May 2025 UK-EU 
summit produced an SPS agreement (food standards), Security and Defence 
Partnership, Erasmus+ rejoining, and fishing rights extended to 2038. 
Current reset commitments add approximately 0.3% to UK GDP by 2040. 
But Labour's red lines — no single market, no customs union, no freedom 
of movement — are "remarkably similar to Theresa May's 2017 position" 
(Bruegel). The Centre for European Reform concludes Labour was 
"disappointingly willing to tolerate continuing economic damage" in 
2025 to avoid accusations of "betraying Brexit."

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative (2024):** Maintain Brexit settlement. No closer EU 
alignment. Independent trade policy. CPTPP. New FTAs globally.

**Labour:** Reset with EU within red lines (no SM, no CU, no FoM). 
SPS agreement. Mutual recognition of professional qualifications. 
Security pact. Starmer now argues for "closer alignment with the 
single market" sector-by-sector (January 2026) but EU unlikely to 
offer this without budget contributions and freedom of movement.

**Liberal Democrats:** Staged approach to rebuilding EU relationship. 
Now calling for bespoke UK-EU customs union. Long-term objective: 
rejoin single market. UK-EU youth mobility scheme.

**Green:** Rejoin EU "as soon as political will is present."

**Reform UK:** "Grasp opportunities of Brexit." Scrap remaining 
EU-derived legislation. Renegotiate or partially abandon post-Brexit 
UK-EU treaties.

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Has Brexit been economically damaging?

**YES. OVERWHELMINGLY.**

OBR: 4% GDP reduction long-term. NBER (Bloom et al., Nov 2025): 
6-8% GDP reduction already realised. Most affected sectors: agrifood, 
textiles, material-based manufacturing (Du et al., 2024). Small firms 
disproportionately hit by fixed costs of customs compliance. British 
Chambers of Commerce (December 2025): "EU trade getting harder."

Services trade also affected: Centre for Economic Performance (June 
2025) found "deglobalisation in disguise" — Brexit barriers reducing 
trade in services, which is the UK's comparative advantage.

**Verdict: CONFIRMED. Damage is measured and ongoing.**
**Confidence: HIGHEST.**

### Assumption 2: Can FTAs compensate for lost EU access?

**NO. NOT EVEN CLOSE.**

CPTPP: 0.06% GDP. Australia FTA: 0.08%. Even with hypothetical US 
and India FTAs, total FTA benefit < 1% GDP. The 4-8% lost from the 
single market cannot be recovered from more distant, less integrated 
trade relationships. Trade intensity declines with distance (gravity 
model — the most robust finding in trade economics).

UK-India trade deal signed July 2025: projected £6bn investment 
and export wins. Meaningful but a fraction of EU trade (£358bn in 
2024, 41% of all UK exports).

**Verdict: FTAs ARE USEFUL BUT DO NOT COMPENSATE.** 
**Confidence: HIGHEST.**

### Assumption 3: Is the "reset" sufficient?

**NO. IT RECOVERS ~0.3% OF A 4-8% LOSS.**

Current reset commitments (SPS deal, electricity alignment, sector 
measures) add ~0.3% to GDP by 2040. This is significant but represents 
less than one-tenth of the estimated damage. The UK in a Changing 
Europe concludes: deeper integration across more sectors would yield 
"considerably greater economic benefits" but requires concessions 
Labour has ruled out — budget contributions and freedom of movement.

The Swiss model is the closest precedent: sector-specific alignment 
with the EU single market in exchange for dynamic regulatory alignment 
and CJEU oversight. The recently renegotiated Swiss-EU deal (2026) 
shows both the potential and the price.

**Verdict: RESET IS NECESSARY BUT INSUFFICIENT. The red lines 
limit economic recovery.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 4: Would a customs union help?

**YES, FOR GOODS. BUT LOSES INDEPENDENT TRADE POLICY.**

A customs union eliminates border checks on goods and removes the 
need for rules of origin. It would address the agrifood, manufacturing, 
and supply chain disruption that Brexit created. But it means adopting 
the EU's common external tariff and losing the ability to negotiate 
independent FTAs (which, as established, are worth < 1% of GDP anyway).

Starmer has argued that single market alignment is preferable to a 
customs union because it preserves trade policy independence. This is 
logically coherent but the EU is unlikely to offer sector-by-sector 
single market access without the responsibilities of membership 
(budget, free movement, CJEU oversight).

Reeves (January 2026): rejoining customs union "would mean losing 
the benefit of trade deals." This is technically true but the benefit 
of those deals (< 1%) is far smaller than the cost of being outside 
the customs union (portion of the 4-8% GDP loss).

**Verdict: CUSTOMS UNION IS NET POSITIVE for goods trade. The 
trade-off (losing FTA independence) is smaller than presented.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Is freedom of movement the binding constraint?

**POLITICALLY YES. ECONOMICALLY, THE MOST COSTLY RED LINE.**

Freedom of movement is the EU's condition for full single market 
access. It is also Labour's firmest red line. The economic evidence: 
free movement enabled UK services exports (the UK's comparative 
advantage), facilitated NHS and social care staffing (cross-domain 
→ Immigration, NHS), and supported sectors from hospitality to 
agriculture.

The Swiss precedent: meaningful single market access requires some 
form of people mobility. The EU will not unbundle this. Starmer's 
attempt to get single market alignment without free movement is 
"yet another case of Brexit wishful thinking" (UK in a Changing Europe).

**Verdict: FREE MOVEMENT IS THE KEY THAT UNLOCKS ECONOMIC RECOVERY. 
Maintaining the red line accepts permanent economic damage.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

This is the domain where evidence and political reality are most 
in tension. The evidence is unambiguous. The politics are binding.

### What the evidence says:

The economically optimal position is single market and customs union 
membership — either through EU membership or an EEA/EFTA-style 
arrangement. This would recover the bulk of the 4-8% GDP loss and 
resolve the trade, services, workforce, and supply chain problems 
identified across every other domain.

### What is politically achievable now:

1. Complete the SPS agreement (in negotiation — target 2027)
2. Join EU electricity market (cross-domain → Energy)
3. Mutual recognition of professional qualifications
4. Youth mobility scheme (EU pressing for this)
5. Defence and security partnership (agreed May 2025)
6. Rejoin Erasmus+ (agreed)
7. Link emissions trading systems (cross-domain → Energy CBAM)

### What should be the medium-term trajectory:

8. Customs union (Lib Dem position — evidence supports this)
9. Regulatory alignment in key sectors (Swiss model)
10. Financial contributions to EU budget proportional to access
11. Youth/worker mobility scheme (not full free movement initially)

### What should be the long-term goal:

12. Single market membership (EEA/EFTA or equivalent)
13. This requires accepting freedom of movement, CJEU oversight, 
    and budget contributions
14. The evidence is unambiguous that this is the economically 
    optimal position
15. State this honestly even if current politics don't allow it

### The assumptions:

1. The EU will negotiate sector-specific agreements (uncertain — 
   EU resists cherry-picking)
2. SPS agreement adds meaningful GDP (estimated but uncertain)
3. Customs union is achievable without full EU membership
4. Public opinion continues shifting toward closer EU ties
5. FTAs with non-EU partners are maintained alongside closer EU alignment
6. Freedom of movement red line will eventually soften
7. Swiss model is replicable for UK (larger economy, more complex)
8. Political consensus for closer EU ties can be built over 10 years

### International precedent:

- **Norway (EEA):** Full single market access. Pays into EU budget. 
  Accepts most EU regulation. No customs union. No say in rules. 
  GDP per capita: higher than UK. Demonstrates that non-membership 
  market access works.
- **Switzerland:** Bilateral agreements. Sector-specific access. 
  Dynamic alignment required. Recently renegotiated (2026). Shows 
  both potential and constraints of the "close but outside" model.
- **Ukraine:** Applied for EU membership during war. Association 
  Agreement with single market alignment. Demonstrates that EU 
  integration can be rapid when political will exists.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs of closer EU alignment:**
- Budget contributions: £5-10bn/yr (EEA-level)
- Regulatory alignment: reduced legislative autonomy
- Freedom of movement: immigration policy constraint
- Political: breaking manifesto red lines

**Benefits:**
- OBR: 4% GDP recovery = ~£100bn/yr
- NBER: 6-8% GDP recovery = ~£150-200bn/yr
- Services trade recovery (UK's comparative advantage)
- NHS/social care workforce (cross-domain → Immigration, NHS)
- Reduced trade friction for 41% of UK exports
- Energy interconnection (cross-domain → Energy)
- Defence cooperation (cross-domain → Defence)

The cost-benefit is overwhelming. The budget contribution 
(£5-10bn) to recover £100-200bn in GDP is the highest-return 
investment in the entire manifesto. No other single policy 
comes close.

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. The GDP estimates may be too high.** The 4-8% range includes 
model uncertainty. Some of the decline may be attributable to 
other factors (pandemic, global trade disruption). The 
counterfactual UK-in-EU is uncertain.

**2. EU membership/EEA may not be on offer.** The EU may not want 
the UK back on terms the UK would accept. Accession would require 
accepting the euro commitment (with opt-out negotiation), Schengen, 
and full acquis. EEA/EFTA membership requires consent of existing 
EFTA members (Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein).

**3. The political window may never open.** Brexit identity politics 
may persist for a generation. If so, the evidence-based position is 
correct but permanently undeliverable. The platform's role is to 
make the evidence impossible to ignore even when politics can't act 
on it.

**4. UK regulatory divergence may have some benefits.** The ability 
to regulate AI, biotech, and financial services independently may 
produce advantages in specific sectors. These are real but small 
relative to the aggregate trade loss.

**5. Global trade patterns may shift.** If the EU's relative economic 
weight declines and Asian/African markets grow, the cost of being 
outside the EU diminishes over time. But gravity model evidence 
suggests geographic proximity remains dominant for decades.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. Honest Public Education on Brexit Impact
- **What:** Publish an annual government report quantifying the 
  economic impact of Brexit — similar to the OBR's fiscal outlook 
  but for trade. Make the cost visible, not hidden.
- **Confidence:** HIGH on need. LOW on political feasibility.

### 2. Customs Union as Stepping Stone
- **What:** Negotiate a bespoke customs union (Lib Dem proposal) as 
  an interim step toward deeper integration. Eliminates border checks 
  on goods. Surrenders independent FTA policy (worth < 1% GDP) to 
  recover goods trade access (worth far more).
- **Confidence:** HIGH on economics. MODERATE on politics.

### 3. Youth Mobility Scheme
- **What:** Accept the EU's youth mobility proposal (18-30, time-
  limited). Not free movement but a step toward normalised people 
  mobility. Benefits UK universities, cultural exchange, and 
  workforce flexibility.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. EU wants this. UK resistance is political 
  not economic.

### 4. Dynamic Regulatory Alignment Framework
- **What:** Establish a legal framework for automatic UK adoption 
  of EU single market rules in agreed sectors (food safety, 
  chemicals, pharmaceuticals, professional qualifications). Reduces 
  bilateral negotiation overhead. Swiss model.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Requires sovereignty concession.
