# HOUSING
## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

---

## The Problem

The UK has a housing crisis with three measurable dimensions.

**Undersupply.** The UK is missing approximately 4.3 million homes 
(Centre for Cities, 2023). Net additional dwellings in England were 
208,600 in 2024/25, a 6% decline. Completions in H1 2025 were 70,670 
— down 12.6% year-on-year. London had just 731 private housing starts 
in H1 2025. Planning permissions hit record lows in Q2 2025 (42,239 
approved, down 19%). The government's target is 300,000/year. Current 
delivery is roughly two-thirds of that. Savills forecasts 840,000 
completions by 2028/29 — 42% short of the 1.5 million target.

**Affordability.** Housing costs consume a larger share of income in the 
UK than in almost any comparable economy. The average house price to 
earnings ratio has roughly doubled since the 1990s. This is primarily a 
London and South East problem but has spread to other prosperous cities 
(Oxford, Cambridge, Bristol, Edinburgh). The private rental sector is in 
crisis: record numbers of households (110,000+) in temporary accommodation, 
costing councils £1.7bn/year.

**Quality.** The UK has the oldest and least energy-efficient housing stock 
in Europe. Millions of homes are below EPC C. Poor insulation drives both 
fuel poverty (cross-domain → Energy, Welfare) and respiratory illness 
(cross-domain → NHS). The Grenfell disaster exposed systemic failures 
in building safety regulation.

The honest framing: the UK has underbuilt homes for 80 years. 
Housebuilding rates dropped by more than a third after the introduction 
of the Town and Country Planning Act 1947, from 2% growth per year 
(1856-1939) to 1.2% (1947-2019). Every government since has failed to 
close the gap. Government spending on housing in England fell 45% in 
real terms between 2009/10 and 2015/16 and remained 20% lower in 
2022/23. This is the primary driver — not immigration, not planning 
alone, not any single policy failure.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** 1.6 million homes over the parliament. No mandatory 
local targets (abandoned in 2023). Help to Buy extension. Mortgage 
Guarantee Scheme. Stamp duty relief for first-time buyers to £425,000. 
Right to Buy maintained. New social housing eligibility tests (local 
connection, UK connection). Abolish Section 21 (via Renters Reform Bill). 
Brownfield priority.

**Labour:** 1.5 million homes. Restore mandatory local housing targets. 
Update NPPF. Planning reform: new generation of New Towns, release 
"grey belt" land with 50% affordable, strategic planning for Combined 
Authorities. "Biggest increase in social and affordable housebuilding 
in a generation." Review Right to Buy discounts. Abolish Section 21 
immediately. £39bn Social and Affordable Homes Programme 2026-2036 
(announced Jan 2026, 60% target for social rent). Fund planning officers 
via non-UK resident stamp duty surcharge.

**Liberal Democrats:** 380,000 homes/year (most ambitious target), 
including 150,000 social homes. Ten new garden cities. Community-led 
development. Abolish residential leasehold. End Right to Buy locally. 
Restore EPC C requirement for landlords. End fuel poverty. Local 
authorities can end Right to Buy entirely.

**Green:** 150,000 social homes/year. "Right Homes, Right Place, Right 
Price" charter. £29bn over 5 years to raise EPC to B. Rent controls. 
Councils can requisition empty/poorly-insulated properties. Birthright 
to housing. Local authority-led insulation programme.

**Reform UK:** Review planning to fast-track brownfield. Tax incentives 
for brownfield. "Loose fit planning" for large developments. Reform 
social housing law — prioritise local people. "Foreign nationals to the 
back of the queue." Scrap Renters Reform Bill. No targets stated.

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Can the UK build 300,000 homes/year?

**NOT WITH CURRENT POLICY. SYSTEMIC REFORM NEEDED.**

The highest post-war completion rate was 243,000 in 2019/20. The 
planning system has never delivered 300,000 consistently. Centre 
for Cities analysis of 80 years of data shows that even at peak 
private sector performance, the target falls 388,000 short over 
five years. Savills forecasts 840,000 by 2028/29 (42% short).

The constraint is not just planning: it's financial viability (costs 
exceed sale prices in many areas), construction workforce capacity, 
supply chain bottleneck, and demand weakness (housebuilders only build 
where they see buyers). Labour's planning reforms are necessary but 
not sufficient.

Historic precedent: 300,000+/year was achieved in the 1950s-1970s 
only when councils built at scale alongside the private sector. 
The private sector has never delivered 300,000 alone.

**Verdict: NOT ACHIEVABLE without major public sector housebuilding 
alongside planning reform.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Is the planning system the main barrier?

**A MAJOR BARRIER BUT NOT THE ONLY ONE.**

Centre for Cities: housebuilding rates dropped >1/3 after the 1947 
Act. The discretionary system (case-by-case decisions vs rules-based 
zoning) creates uncertainty, delay, and cost. Consents now take 18+ 
months (double pre-2020). Only 42,239 homes approved in Q2 2025.

But the Resolution Foundation and others note that even with planning 
permission, developers only build when demand supports sales. 
"Land-banking" is partly a function of the business model — large 
housebuilders release homes at a rate that maintains prices.

International comparison: countries with rules-based zoning (Germany, 
Japan, most of Europe) build more housing more consistently. Centre 
for Cities advocates replacing discretionary planning with European-
style zoning. This is the most radical proposal in the evidence base 
and no party has adopted it.

**Verdict: PLANNING IS NECESSARY BUT NOT SUFFICIENT.** Demand, finance, 
and construction capacity are co-constraints. 
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 3: Does immigration drive the housing crisis?

**IMMIGRATION ADDS DEMAND BUT UNDERSUPPLY IS THE PRIMARY DRIVER.**

The 4.3 million home deficit existed before the post-2021 migration 
surge. Government spending on housing fell 45% in real terms during 
austerity. The undersupply is concentrated in prosperous cities 
(London, Oxford, Cambridge, Bristol) where housing costs are highest 
regardless of immigration levels.

Immigration does add to demand — but so do smaller household sizes, 
longer life expectancy, and internal migration to cities. Blaming 
immigration for housing costs is factually misleading when the 
fundamental problem is 80 years of underbuilding.

**Verdict: UNDERSUPPLY IS PRIMARY. Immigration is a secondary factor.**
**Confidence: HIGH.** (Cross-domain → Immigration A8.)

### Assumption 4: Does Right to Buy help or hurt?

**HELPED INDIVIDUALS. DEVASTATED SOCIAL HOUSING STOCK.**

Since 1980, ~2 million council homes sold under Right to Buy. Only 
a fraction replaced. Social housing stock has declined from 5.5m to 
~4m. The 2012 increase in discounts accelerated sales without 
accelerating replacement. Receipts from sales were restricted — 
councils couldn't use them to build replacements 1:1.

Labour's £39bn SAHP (2026-2036) with 60% social rent target is the 
first attempt at scale replacement in decades. But even 180,000 
social rent homes over 10 years doesn't close the gap from 2m sold.

**Verdict: RIGHT TO BUY NEEDS FUNDAMENTAL REFORM.** At minimum, 
1:1 replacement requirement. Lib Dem position (local authority power 
to end RTB) is evidence-consistent. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Do rent controls work?

**EVIDENCE IS MIXED AND CONTEXT-DEPENDENT.**

The academic evidence on rent controls is among the most contested 
in economics. Short-term: rent controls protect existing tenants 
from price shocks. Long-term: strict controls (first-generation, 
fixed nominal prices) reduce supply, quality, and mobility. 
Second-generation controls (inflation-linked caps, new-let 
exemptions) have more moderate effects.

International evidence: Berlin reintroduced rent controls in 2020 
then partially struck down by court in 2021. Scotland introduced a 
rent cap in 2022 — evidence on impact still limited. Sweden's 
long-standing controls have created a parallel market and long queues.

The UK's problem is primarily supply, not price regulation. Rent 
controls without supply increase merely redistribute scarcity.

**Verdict: NOT A PRIMARY SOLUTION.** May be useful as a short-term 
stabiliser alongside massive supply increase, but cannot substitute 
for building more homes. Green position (rent controls) addresses 
symptom, not cause. **Confidence: MEDIUM.**

### Assumption 6: Can the private sector deliver at scale alone?

**NO. HISTORIC EVIDENCE IS UNAMBIGUOUS.**

The private sector peaked at ~200,000 completions/year in the 
late 2000s. 300,000+/year was only achieved with major public 
sector building in the 1950s-1970s (councils built 100,000+/year). 
The current large housebuilder model (5 companies control ~50% of 
output) creates structural constraints: developers release homes 
at rates that maintain prices.

The market alone will not solve a housing crisis because the crisis 
itself (high prices) is profitable for incumbents. Diversification 
is essential: councils, housing associations, community-led schemes, 
self-builders, Build to Rent, Modern Methods of Construction.

**Verdict: PUBLIC SECTOR BUILDING ESSENTIAL alongside private.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 7: Does building more homes actually reduce prices?

**YES, BUT SLOWLY AND AT SCALE.**

The Bank of England, IFS, and Resolution Foundation all find that 
increased supply moderates prices — but the effect is gradual. House 
prices are driven by land values, credit availability, interest rates, 
and expectations, not just physical supply. Building 300,000/year for 
a decade would moderate price growth rather than crash prices.

The Resolution Foundation estimates using housebuilding to moderate 
prices is "a long-term project that requires consistently higher levels 
of supply" — not a quick fix.

**Verdict: SUPPLY REDUCES PRICES BUT OVER DECADES, NOT YEARS.**
**Confidence: HIGH on direction, MEDIUM on magnitude.**

### Assumption 8: Is the quality of existing stock a crisis?

**YES. MEASURABLY.**

The UK has the oldest housing stock in Europe. Millions of homes are 
below EPC C. The Green Homes Grant (2020-2021) delivered only 10% of 
target (cross-domain → Energy E19). Cold homes drive fuel poverty and 
respiratory illness. Grenfell exposed systemic building safety failures.

The CCC identifies insulation as the most cost-effective carbon 
abatement measure — at negative cost over building lifetime 
(cross-domain → Energy E20, P07). Every £1 spent on insulation 
saves more than £1 in energy costs and health costs.

**Verdict: QUALITY IS AS IMPORTANT AS QUANTITY.** Retrofit is the 
highest-ROI housing investment. **Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The policy:

Build at scale through planning reform AND public sector delivery, 
while retrofitting the existing stock as the highest-ROI investment 
available — funded by capturing a share of the land value uplift that 
planning permission creates.

### The mechanism:

**Supply:**
- Restore mandatory local housing targets (LAB — done)
- Replace discretionary planning with rules-based zoning over 10 years 
  (Centre for Cities recommendation — no party proposes this fully)
- Revive council housebuilding to 50,000+/year alongside housing 
  associations and private sector
- £39bn SAHP is the right scale — hold Labour to the 60% social rent 
  target
- New Towns programme with genuine infrastructure (not just greenfield 
  estates without services)
- Modern Methods of Construction: prefabricated/modular housing to 
  reduce build times and costs
- Community Land Trusts for permanently affordable housing

**Affordability:**
- Reform Right to Buy: 1:1 replacement requirement nationally, local 
  authority power to suspend in areas of acute shortage
- Reform land compensation: capture a larger share of planning uplift 
  for public benefit (currently developers capture most of the value 
  that public planning permission creates)
- Build to Rent at scale with long-term institutional investment 
  (pension funds, insurance) — not just build-to-sell
- Social rent as the primary affordable tenure (not "affordable rent" 
  at 80% market rate, which is unaffordable in high-cost areas)

**Quality:**
- National Insulation Service (cross-domain → Energy P07): 
  institutional delivery of retrofit, 1 million homes/year
- EPC C minimum for all rented properties by 2028 (Lib Dem position)
- Implement Grenfell recommendations in full
- Awaab's Law extended to private sector (LAB — committed)

**Renters:**
- Abolish Section 21 (all parties except Reform)
- Proper enforcement of existing standards (councils lack 
  enforcement capacity — needs funding)
- Rent stabilisation mechanism for sitting tenants (inflation-linked 
  cap, not absolute control) as interim measure while supply increases

### The assumptions:

1. Planning reform increases consented land over 3-5 years
2. Public sector can scale to 50,000+ homes/year (requires workforce 
   and funding — both uncertain)
3. £39bn SAHP is sustained over full 10-year term
4. Land value capture reform is politically achievable
5. Construction workforce can expand (currently shrinking)
6. Interest rates stabilise to make development viable
7. Retrofit delivery body can scale (cross-domain → Energy A12)
8. Public accepts development in their area (NIMBY constraint)

### International precedent:

- **Vienna:** 62% of population lives in subsidised housing. 
  City builds and owns housing at scale. Rents stable for decades. 
  Proves public-sector-led housing can work at city scale.
- **Japan:** Rules-based zoning nationwide. Housing is ~5x more 
  affordable relative to income than UK equivalent cities. Proves 
  planning system design directly affects affordability.
- **Singapore:** 80% of population in public housing (HDB). Built 
  a nation's housing stock in two generations. Extreme case but 
  proves public sector can deliver at enormous scale.
- **Netherlands:** Social housing associations provide ~30% of all 
  housing. Mixed-income developments prevent segregation. Strong 
  tenant protections co-exist with adequate supply.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- £39bn SAHP over 10 years (committed)
- Council housebuilding ramp-up: £5-10bn/year additional
- Planning system reform: £200m/year (planning officers, digital systems)
- Retrofit/insulation: £3bn+/year (cross-domain → Energy)
- Enforcement of rental standards: £500m/year

**Hard benefits:**
- Each new home generates ~£50,000 in economic activity during 
  construction (HBF estimate)
- Reduced temporary accommodation: 110,000 households × £20,000/yr 
  average = £2.2bn/year potential saving
- Social rent homes reduce housing benefit expenditure vs private rent
- Insulation: £300-600/year per home in energy savings (cross-domain)

**Proxy-monetised benefits:**
- Health: warm, dry, secure homes reduce NHS costs (respiratory, 
  mental health). NICE estimates £1 spent on housing adaptations 
  saves £2-7 in health/social care costs.
- Productivity: long commutes (driven by unaffordable housing near 
  jobs) reduce economic output. Agglomeration benefits of affordable 
  housing in productive cities.
- Children: overcrowding affects educational outcomes. Stable housing 
  improves school attendance.

**Sensitivity:**
- If interest rates stay high: development viability worsens, more 
  public subsidy needed. Direction unchanged.
- If construction workforce doesn't expand: delivery timeline extends 
  but need remains. Invest in training and MMC.
- If land value capture politically blocked: funding model weakened 
  but £39bn SAHP provides alternative.

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. The 300,000 target may be wrong.** Some academics argue the 4.3m 
deficit figure is contested and that 300,000/year overshoots actual need 
in some regions while undershooting in London. The target may need to be 
regional, not national.

**2. Zoning could create its own problems.** Rules-based planning works 
in Japan and Germany in very different cultural and institutional contexts. 
The UK's relationship with property, land ownership, and local democracy 
is different. Imposing zoning on a system built around discretion could 
create transition chaos.

**3. Public sector delivery may not scale.** Council housebuilding 
collapsed in the 1980s and the institutional capacity hasn't been rebuilt. 
Workforce, procurement expertise, and project management capability 
don't exist at the required scale. Rebuilding them takes years.

**4. Building more homes won't solve affordability if land prices 
don't change.** Housing costs are primarily land costs. Building more 
homes on expensive land produces expensive homes. Without land value 
capture or public land release at below-market rates, new supply may 
not be affordable.

**5. The construction industry may be structurally incapable.** The 
Farmer Review (2016) described the industry as a "dying patient." 
Skills shortages, low productivity, and dominance by a few large 
firms limit capacity. Modern Methods of Construction could help but 
adoption is slow.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. Land Value Capture Reform
- **What:** When planning permission is granted, land values increase 
  10-100x. Currently developers capture most of this. Reform compulsory 
  purchase to enable acquisition at closer to existing use value, 
  capturing the uplift for public benefit (infrastructure, affordable 
  homes).
- **Evidence:** Centre for Cities, IPPR, and Shelter all advocate this. 
  The Town and Country Planning Association has campaigned for it since 
  the 1947 Act. International precedent: Netherlands, Singapore, Hong 
  Kong all capture land value uplift for public housing.
- **Estimated impact:** Could fund 50,000+ additional affordable homes 
  per year from uplift alone. 
- **Risk:** Legally complex. Developers will resist. Constitutional 
  questions about property rights.
- **Confidence:** HIGH that it would work. LOW on political feasibility.

### 2. Rules-Based Zoning (10-Year Transition)
- **What:** Replace the discretionary planning system with European-style 
  zoning. Local plans define what can be built where. If a proposal 
  conforms to the zone, it gets permission automatically. Removes the 
  case-by-case uncertainty that adds cost, time, and risk.
- **Evidence:** Centre for Cities' 80-year dataset shows discretionary 
  planning has suppressed housebuilding since 1947. Japan's zoning 
  system delivers affordable housing in the world's most expensive 
  city (Tokyo). Germany, Netherlands, most of Europe use zoning.
- **Risk:** Transition period would be chaotic. Local democratic input 
  shifts from individual decisions to plan-making (some would see this 
  as a loss of control; others as more democratic).
- **Confidence:** HIGH that it increases supply. MODERATE on UK 
  deliverability.

### 3. National Housing Bank
- **What:** A public institution providing low-cost, long-term finance 
  for social housing construction. Similar to Germany's KfW or 
  Canada's CMHC. Borrows at government rates, lends to councils and 
  housing associations at below-market rates.
- **Evidence:** The government announced £20bn+ in "patient capital" 
  via Homes England and a National Housing Bank concept. This is the 
  right direction. KfW has financed millions of German homes and 
  retrofits. The UK has no equivalent institution.
- **Estimated impact:** £20bn in patient capital could leverage £53bn 
  private investment (government estimate). Reduces borrowing costs 
  for social housing providers by 1-2%.
- **Risk:** Fiscal rules may constrain government balance sheet. 
  Requires sustained multi-year commitment.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Proven international model.

### 4. Modern Methods of Construction Mandate
- **What:** Require a minimum percentage (20-30%) of publicly funded 
  new homes to use MMC (prefabricated, modular, offsite construction). 
  Creates guaranteed demand to justify factory investment.
- **Evidence:** MMC can reduce build times by 30-50% and costs by 
  10-20%. The UK has piloted MMC but adoption remains low because 
  of uncertain demand. A mandate creates the market signal needed 
  for investment. Sweden builds ~90% of homes with MMC.
- **Risk:** Quality concerns if scaled too fast. Factory investment 
  requires confidence in sustained demand.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE-HIGH.

### 5. Community Land Trusts at Scale
- **What:** CLTs own land in perpetuity and lease it for housing. 
  Homes remain affordable forever because the land cost is removed. 
  Currently tiny in the UK (~300 CLTs).
- **Evidence:** Strong US precedent (Champlain Housing Trust, Burlington). 
  UK CLTs have successfully delivered permanently affordable homes but 
  at tiny scale. Government allocated £20m — needs 100x more.
- **Risk:** Scaling requires land at affordable prices (back to land 
  value capture). Complex governance.
- **Confidence:** HIGH that the model works. LOW on scale without 
  policy support.
