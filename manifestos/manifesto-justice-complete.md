## The Problem

The UK justice system has been "allowed to grind to a halt" (Labour 
manifesto). Three measurable dimensions:

**The courts are collapsing.** The Crown Court backlog is at record 
levels and growing quarter on quarter. Even with record investment 
(£287m in court estate, £126m more per year in criminal legal aid), 
the backlog is projected to reach 113,000 by 2029 and could rise to 
174,000 by 2035 (MoJ). Without action: over 200,000 by 2035. Mean 
case time in magistrates' courts: 6.5 months from offence to justice 
(2024) — 42% longer than 2010. Every stage is slower: offence to 
charge, charge to hearing, hearing to completion.

**Prisons are full.** The prison population is near capacity. 20% 
of prisoners are on remand (awaiting trial) — a direct consequence 
of the court backlog. The government has already implemented 
emergency early release schemes. Four new prisons (20,000 places) 
are planned but won't solve the structural problem: the system 
sends too many people to prison for too long, while failing to 
rehabilitate them. 48% reoffend within a year of release.

**Legal aid has been gutted.** Legal aid funding was cut by £1bn/year 
under the Legal Aid, Sentencing and Punishment of Offenders Act 2012 
(LASPO). Access to justice has become a function of wealth. Tim Owen KC 
noted that Labour's manifesto did not include a commitment to increase 
funding for courts or legal aid. The IFS echoed this: "How would 
either party deal with backlogs in the court system, overflowing 
prisons? We have not a clue."

The honest framing: the justice system was systematically defunded 
during austerity and has never recovered. Court buildings are crumbling, 
barrister and judge recruitment is in crisis, prison officers are 
leaving faster than they can be hired, and the system produces 
failure at every stage: slow justice, overcrowded prisons, high 
reoffending, and victims who wait years for resolution. The IFS puts 
justice alongside education, social care, and local government as 
a domain where no party has stated how they would fund recovery.

**Conservative:** Build four new prisons (20,000 places). Tougher 
sentences for knife crime, grooming, retail assault. Continue legal 
aid. Cut Covid court backlog via Nightingale courts. Joint enterprise 
doctrine maintained. Mandatory life for repeat violent offenders.

**Labour:** Review sentencing. Specialist rape courts. Extend 
Associate Prosecutor powers. Hillsborough Law (duty of candour). 
Neighbourhood policing. Youth violence prevention (public health 
approach). Respect Orders for antisocial behaviour. Strategic 
probation review. Early release to manage prison capacity. No 
commitment to increase court/legal aid funding.

**Liberal Democrats:** Most comprehensive justice platform. End prison 
overcrowding. Recruit/retain prison officers. Women's Justice Board. 
Replace Young Offender Institutions with Secure Schools. Statutory 
youth diversion. "Through the gate" mentorship for prison leavers. 
National Resettlement Plan. Right to affordable legal assistance. 
Reform drug laws. Devolution of justice to Wales.

**Green:** £11bn to restore legal aid. £2.5bn to repair courts. 
Recruit judges. Presumption against prison sentences under 2 years. 
Rights of Nature Act. Scrap Public Order Act. Defend Human Rights 
Act and ECHR. Drug law reform (regulated market). Separate immigration 
from criminal justice (abolish Home Office jurisdiction).

**Reform UK:** Zero tolerance policing. Mandatory jail for all violent 
crime and knife possession. Mandatory life for drug dealing/trafficking. 
Automatic life for repeat violent offenders. Deport foreign criminals 
immediately. High-intensity training camps for young offenders. Abolish 
DEI roles. Change hate crime definition.

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Does building more prisons solve the crisis?

**NO. THE SYSTEM HAS A DEMAND PROBLEM, NOT JUST A CAPACITY PROBLEM.**

England and Wales imprison more people per capita than almost any 
Western European country. The prison population has roughly doubled 
since 1990 — driven by longer sentences, not more crime. Crime is at 
historic lows. Building more prisons without addressing sentencing 
policy is treating the symptom. The Centre for Justice Innovation 
calls for "resetting the automatic release point" and reducing short 
sentences that produce high reoffending.

**Verdict: PRISONS ARE A SYMPTOM. Sentencing reform is the cause.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Does tougher sentencing reduce crime?

**NO CONSISTENT EVIDENCE.**

The criminological evidence is clear: certainty of detection reduces 
crime far more than severity of punishment. Longer sentences don't 
deter — most offenders don't rationally calculate sentence length 
before offending. What works: visible policing (neighbourhood 
policing), swift justice (reducing time from offence to court), 
and rehabilitation that addresses root causes (addiction, mental 
health, housing, employment).

Reform UK's "zero tolerance" and mandatory sentences would massively 
increase the prison population without reducing crime. The US 
experience with mass incarceration demonstrates this definitively.

**Verdict: CERTAINTY OF DETECTION > SEVERITY OF PUNISHMENT.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 3: Is legal aid a luxury or a necessity?

**A NECESSITY. ACCESS TO JUSTICE IS A CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHT.**

LASPO (2012) cut £1bn/year from legal aid. The result: people 
representing themselves in court (increasing delays and errors), 
family law disputes unresolved (affecting children), housing 
evictions unchallenged (cross-domain → Housing), immigration cases 
unrepresented (cross-domain → Immigration), and domestic abuse 
victims unable to access protection. The Green proposal (£11bn 
restoration) is the most ambitious. The Lib Dem proposal (right to 
affordable legal assistance) creates a statutory framework.

**Verdict: LEGAL AID IS INFRASTRUCTURE, NOT CHARITY.**
**Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 4: Should drug policy be reformed?

**THE EVIDENCE OVERWHELMINGLY SUPPORTS REFORM.**

Portugal decriminalised personal drug use in 2001: HIV infections 
fell 95%, overdose deaths fell below EU average, drug-related 
crime fell. The UK's punitive drug policy fills prisons, costs 
billions in enforcement, and fails to reduce use. The Centre for 
Justice Innovation calls for an independent commission on drug 
policy. The Lib Dems and Greens support reform. Labour and 
Conservatives don't touch it.

**Verdict: DRUG POLICY REFORM IS EVIDENCE-BASED. Current policy 
is evidence-free.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Can the court backlog be cleared?

**NOT WITHOUT SYSTEMIC INVESTMENT AND REFORM.**

Even with record investment, MoJ projects the backlog reaching 
113,000 by 2029. The IfG (2025): "Backlogs continue to grow quarter 
on quarter and long-term reforms to get the system back on a 
sustainable footing have yet to be announced." The problems cascade: 
court backlog → remand population grows → prison overcrowding → 
early release → reoffending → more court cases.

The Centre for Justice Innovation proposes an "Institute for Justice" 
— annual independent forecasts of criminal justice capacity and 
demand (an OBR for justice). This is exactly the platform concept 
applied to criminal justice governance.

**Verdict: SYSTEMIC REFORM NEEDED. Current trajectory is 
unsustainable.** **Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The mechanism:

**Courts:**
- Sustained investment in court estate, digital systems, sitting days
- Recruit more judges and criminal barristers (workforce crisis)
- Fast-track specialist courts: rape, domestic abuse, fraud
- Digital case management to reduce delays
- Institute for Justice: independent capacity/demand forecasting

**Sentencing:**
- Sentencing review (Labour committed — hold them to it)
- Presumption against short sentences (under 12 months) — evidence 
  shows they increase reoffending without reducing crime
- Community sentences with robust supervision for lower-risk offenders
- Address IPP (Imprisonment for Public Protection) injustice — 
  thousands still imprisoned beyond tariff

**Prisons:**
- End overcrowding as the binding priority
- Invest in rehabilitation: education, training, employment, 
  addiction treatment, mental health
- "Through the gate" support: housing, employment, benefits arranged 
  before release (Lib Dem model)
- Women's Justice Board (Lib Dem) — women's prisons are particularly 
  failing

**Legal aid:**
- Restore legal aid to pre-LASPO levels phased over parliament
- Priority areas: housing evictions, family law, immigration, 
  domestic abuse
- Hillsborough Law: duty of candour + legal aid for state-related 
  deaths (Labour — committed)

**Policing:**
- Neighbourhood policing restoration (Labour — committed)
- Public health approach to youth violence (evidence-based)
- Youth diversion as statutory duty (Lib Dem)
- Drug policy: independent commission with citizens' jury input

**Drugs:**
- Independent drug policy commission (CJI recommendation)
- Decriminalisation of personal possession as minimum step
- Redirect enforcement spending to treatment and prevention
- Portuguese model as evidence base

### International precedent:

- **Portugal:** Decriminalisation (2001). Drug-related deaths, HIV, 
  and crime all fell. The most successful drug policy reform in 
  Europe. 25 years of evidence.
- **Norway/Finland:** Low imprisonment rates, high rehabilitation 
  investment. Reoffending rates roughly half the UK's. Proves that 
  fewer prisoners + better rehabilitation = less crime.
- **Netherlands:** Has been closing prisons due to falling crime and 
  lower incarceration rates. The opposite trajectory to England.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- Court investment: ~£500m/yr additional
- Legal aid restoration: ~£1bn/yr phased
- Prison rehabilitation: ~£500m/yr
- Neighbourhood policing: ~£1bn/yr (Labour costed)
- Drug treatment expansion: ~£500m/yr
- Total: ~£3-4bn/yr additional

**Benefits:**
- Each reoffender costs ~£65,000/yr in prison + £20,000+ in 
  court/police costs. Reducing reoffending by 10% saves hundreds 
  of millions
- Court backlog reduction: faster justice, fewer remand prisoners, 
  reduced prison costs
- Legal aid: prevents escalation (housing eviction → homelessness 
  → NHS → more welfare spending)
- Drug treatment: £4 return for every £1 invested (Home Office 
  estimate)
- Youth diversion: prevents criminal career formation (Heckman-
  adjacent: early intervention highest ROI)

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. Public wants punishment, not rehabilitation.** Polling 
consistently shows the public believes sentences are too lenient. 
Evidence-based sentencing reform may be politically impossible even 
if technically correct. The platform can state the evidence but 
can't override public preference for punishment.

**2. Drug reform may increase use.** The Portuguese evidence is 
strong but context-specific. The UK's different drug market, 
culture, and policing structure may produce different outcomes. 
An independent commission is the right process to assess this.

**3. Legal aid restoration competes for the same fiscal space.** 
£1bn/year for legal aid is £1bn not available for NHS, education, 
or housing. The cross-domain graph shows this tension — every 
domain competes for constrained resources.

**4. The court system may be structurally unreformable.** The IfG 
identifies fragmented governance as a core problem — no single 
authority controls the whole system. Police, CPS, courts, prisons, 
and probation operate independently with different incentives. 
Coordination reform may be harder than funding reform.
