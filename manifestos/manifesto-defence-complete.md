# DEFENCE & SECURITY
## Evidence-Based Manifesto Section — Complete Draft
## Status: DRAFT v1.0 — 8 April 2026

---

## The Problem

The UK's defence posture is being reshaped by geopolitical realities that 
have moved faster than any manifesto anticipated.

**The threat environment has transformed.** Russia's invasion of Ukraine 
(2022-ongoing) ended three decades of post-Cold War peace in Europe. 
Trump's return to the White House (January 2025) created uncertainty about 
US commitment to NATO's Article 5. The 2025 NATO summit set a 3.5% GDP 
target for core defence spending by 2035, plus 1.5% for resilience — 
totalling 5% of GDP. Iran-related tensions in the Strait of Hormuz have 
demonstrated energy security's military dimension (cross-domain → Energy).

**The armed forces are hollowed out.** The Defence Select Committee (2024) 
questioned the "warfighting readiness" of UK forces and concluded that 
either the MOD must be "fully funded to engage in operations whilst 
developing warfighting readiness" or "the Government must reduce the 
operational burden." Day-to-day spending fell 2.4% in real terms since 
2015/16 while capital rose 95.5% — the military has new equipment but 
fewer people to operate it. UK armed forces personnel have fallen from 
~190,000 to ~140,000 since 2010.

**Spending is rising rapidly and the target keeps moving.** UK defence 
spending was £60.2bn in 2024/25 (2.3% GDP). Labour committed to 2.5% 
by 2027, 3% next parliament. Then NATO's Hague Summit (June 2025) 
agreed 5% by 2035 — 3.5% core military + 1.5% resilience. The UK PM 
claims the UK will reach "at least 4.1% of GDP in 2027" under the new 
two-part definition including security services. S&P Global estimates 
meeting just the 3.5% core target could add ~$2 trillion to European 
NATO members' collective debt by 2035. 5% of UK GDP would be roughly 
£130bn/year — more than double current spending. European allies raised 
spending 20% in a single year (2025). NATO Sec Gen Rutte called for 
a "wartime mindset." Spain refused the 5% target, capping at 2.1%, 
arguing it was "disproportionate and incompatible with a welfare state." 
That is the honest tension no other ally has stated publicly.

## What The Parties Propose

**Conservative:** 2.5% by 2030. Nuclear deterrent maintained. AUKUS. 
New equipment programmes. National Service for school leavers.

**Labour:** SDR (Strategic Defence Review) — externally led by Lord 
Robertson. 2.5% by 2027 (accelerated). 3% in next parliament. Then 
3.5% by 2035 per NATO. Nuclear deterrent. AUKUS. "NATO-first" policy. 
Funded partly by cutting aid from 0.5% to 0.3% of GNI. Concluded SDR 
sets priorities within budget.

**Liberal Democrats:** 2.5% commitment. Review nuclear deterrent 
(not committed to like-for-like Trident replacement). Invest in cyber 
and resilience. Maintain NATO commitment.

**Green:** No spending target. Oppose increases. Redirect military 
spending to public services and climate. No mention of NATO targets.

**Reform UK:** Strong defence posture. Increase military spending. 
National Service. Stronger border enforcement (militarised framing).

## What The Evidence Says

### Assumption 1: Is 2.5% enough?

**NO. ALREADY OVERTAKEN BY EVENTS.**

The 2.5% target was set before the 2025 NATO summit agreed 3.5% core 
+ 1.5% resilience = 5% by 2035. The OBR estimates reaching 3% would 
cost an additional £17.3bn/year by 2029/30. Reaching 5% would roughly 
double current spending. The Defence Select Committee's conclusion — 
fully fund or reduce commitments — remains unresolved.

Germany overtook the UK as NATO's second-highest spender in 2024 
(for the first time), spending $93.7bn with a 24% increase. European 
NATO spending increased 16% in a single year (2023-2024). The UK is 
being outpaced by allies it previously led.

**Verdict: 2.5% IS ALREADY OBSOLETE.** NATO agreed 5% at The Hague. 
3.5% core is the real minimum for credibility. Spain's refusal and 
framing — that 5% is "incompatible with a welfare state" — is the 
most honest statement any ally has made. The UK has not confronted 
this trade-off. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 2: Can defence increases be funded by cutting aid?

**PARTIALLY. CREATES NEW RISKS.**

Labour's plan to cut ODA from 0.5% to 0.3% of GNI provides ~£6.1bn 
for defence. But aid spending addresses the upstream causes of 
instability — failed states, climate displacement, poverty — that 
eventually generate security threats. The military establishment 
itself has historically argued that aid spending is a security 
investment.

**Verdict: SHORT-TERM FIX THAT MAY WORSEN LONG-TERM SECURITY.**
**Confidence: MEDIUM.**

### Assumption 3: Is the nuclear deterrent the right priority?

**STRATEGIC CASE STRONG. FISCAL COST ENORMOUS.**

The nuclear deterrent is the single largest programme cost in the 
defence budget. Trident replacement is essential if the UK maintains 
its nuclear posture — which all parties except potentially the Greens 
and historically the SNP support. The strategic case is strengthened 
by Russian nuclear threats over Ukraine and the uncertain US commitment 
under Trump.

However: nuclear spending crowds out conventional capabilities. The 
UK has fewer ships, fewer aircraft, fewer ground forces than at any 
point since before WWI. A nuclear deterrent that leaves the 
conventional military unable to fight is a strategic imbalance.

**Verdict: MAINTAIN DETERRENT but be honest about the opportunity 
cost.** **Confidence: HIGH on maintaining, MEDIUM on optimal balance.**

### Assumption 4: Is the SDR the right approach?

**YES. FIRST EXTERNALLY-LED REVIEW IS A GENUINE INNOVATION.**

Lord Robertson's SDR is the first UK defence review led by external 
reviewers rather than government. This reduces the risk of institutional 
bias (services protecting their own budgets) and creates space for 
honest prioritisation. The terms of reference acknowledge the need 
for "prioritisation of objectives, and therefore investments."

**Verdict: RIGHT APPROACH.** External review enables honesty that 
internal reviews can't. **Confidence: HIGH.**

### Assumption 5: Should the UK increase conventional readiness or invest in new technology?

**BOTH, BUT THE BALANCE HAS BEEN WRONG.**

Capital spending rose 95.5% since 2015/16 while day-to-day spending 
fell 2.4%. The military has new equipment but insufficient personnel 
to maintain and operate it. Recruitment is in crisis — the armed 
forces are ~15% below target strength.

The evidence from Ukraine: mass still matters (artillery, ammunition, 
drones in volume), but technology (drones, AI, electronic warfare, 
space) is transformative. The UK needs both stockpiles of conventional 
ammunition and investment in autonomous systems, cyber, and AI.

**Verdict: REBALANCE toward personnel AND technology. Current 
capital-heavy bias is unsustainable.** **Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH.**

### Assumption 6: Can defence spending increase without squeezing public services?

**NOT WITHOUT TAX INCREASES.**

The IFS: non-defence departmental spending is planned to be "almost 
flat" between 2026/27 and 2027/28 due to rapid defence spending 
growth. Reaching 3% by next parliament without tax rises means real-
terms cuts to NHS, education, housing, welfare — the same services 
this manifesto identifies as already underfunded.

**Verdict: DEFENCE INCREASES REQUIRE TAXATION REFORM.**
(Cross-domain → Taxation). **Confidence: HIGH.**

## The Evidence-Based Position

### The mechanism:

**Spending:**
- Reach 2.5% by 2027 (committed — hold Labour to it)
- Credible pathway to 3% by 2030
- 3.5% by 2035 per NATO commitment
- Fund through taxation reform (→ Taxation), not service cuts
- Maintain aid at 0.5% GNI — aid is upstream security investment

**Posture:**
- NATO-first (correct and consensus)
- Nuclear deterrent maintained (no credible alternative given current 
  threat environment)
- Rebalance capital vs revenue: personnel and readiness matter as 
  much as new platforms
- Prioritise: drones and autonomous systems, cyber and information 
  operations, ammunition stockpiles, and logistics resilience
- AUKUS maintained (Indo-Pacific strategic hedge)

**Industrial:**
- UK defence industrial base is a strategic asset — maintain 
  sovereign capability in shipbuilding, aerospace, and electronics
- Accelerate procurement reform (current system takes decades)
- Support SME defence sector alongside primes

**Personnel:**
- Address recruitment crisis: pay, housing, conditions of service
- Military housing is a scandal — many service families live in 
  substandard accommodation (cross-domain → Housing)
- Reserve forces expansion as cost-effective complement to regulars
- Veteran support: mental health, transition to civilian employment

### The assumptions:

1. Russia remains a threat through 2030s
2. US commitment to NATO weakens (requiring European self-reliance)
3. China's posture requires Indo-Pacific engagement (AUKUS)
4. Nuclear deterrent remains the appropriate strategic insurance
5. Tax reform generates revenue for defence without cutting services
6. Procurement reform can accelerate delivery
7. Personnel crisis can be solved through conditions improvement
8. Technology (drones, AI, cyber) reshapes warfare faster than 
   current programmes assume

### International precedent:

- **Finland:** Joined NATO 2023. Maintains large, well-equipped 
  reserve force. Defence spending 2.4% GDP with credible territorial 
  defence. Proves small nation can maintain serious capability.
- **Germany:** Zeitenwende — €100bn special fund for defence (2022). 
  Now outspends UK. Shows rapid spending increase is possible when 
  political will exists.
- **Ukraine:** Demonstrates that mass, ammunition, drones, and 
  societal resilience matter as much as expensive platforms. The 
  most relevant current case study for European defence planning.
- **South Korea/Israel:** Technology-forward defence with strong 
  domestic industrial base. Models for UK's niche capabilities.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis:

**Costs:**
- Current spending: ~£60bn/yr (2.3% GDP)
- 2.5% target (2027): ~£65bn/yr
- 3.5% core target (2035): ~£90bn/yr — increase of ~£30bn/yr
- 5% total target (2035): ~£130bn/yr — increase of ~£70bn/yr
- S&P Global: 3.5% alone adds ~$2 trillion to European NATO debt
- If aid maintained at 0.5%: additional £6.1bn needed from elsewhere
- Spain's honest framing: 5% is "incompatible with a welfare state"

**Benefits:**
- Security: deterrence of state aggression (not monetisable but 
  existential)
- Industrial: defence sector employs ~200,000+ and supports 
  supply chains nationwide
- Technology: defence R&D produces spillovers (internet, GPS, 
  jet engines all originated in military research)
- Alliance credibility: UK meeting NATO targets maintains 
  influence and US/European trust

**Sensitivity:**
- If Russia collapses or de-escalates: spending pressure reduces, 
  but China/Iran threats persist
- If US fully commits to NATO: European self-reliance less urgent, 
  but still necessary for independence
- If technology makes mass less relevant: smaller, more 
  tech-heavy force viable (but Ukraine suggests mass still matters)

## Where I Might Be Wrong

**1. 3.5% may not be deliverable alongside other commitments.** 
£30bn/yr additional is enormous. Even with taxation reform, it 
competes with NHS (£20-25bn gap), housing, education. The graph 
shows every domain needs more money. Defence may need to accept 
less than 3.5% if the fiscal position doesn't support it.

**2. The nuclear deterrent may be poor value.** Trident replacement 
costs could fund several conventional capabilities that are more 
usable. The Lib Dem review position deserves serious consideration — 
not because nuclear weapons aren't important, but because the 
opportunity cost should be stated honestly.

**3. European defence integration may be more efficient.** Rather 
than every European country maintaining independent capabilities, 
pooling and specialisation could deliver more capability at lower 
cost. The UK has historically resisted this for sovereignty reasons, 
but the fiscal pressure may make it necessary.

**4. Procurement may be unreformable.** Every defence review since 
the 1960s has identified procurement as broken. None has fixed it. 
The institutional incentives (military services protecting programmes, 
defence industry protecting contracts, politicians protecting 
constituency jobs) may be stronger than any reform.

**5. The Green Party may be right that climate is the bigger 
security threat.** Climate displacement, resource conflict, and 
food insecurity may generate more instability than state aggression. 
If so, spending on climate (→ Energy) is more cost-effective 
security spending than military hardware.

## What Else Should Be Done

### 1. European Defence Cooperation (Beyond NATO)
- **What:** Deeper UK-EU defence cooperation on procurement, R&D, 
  and operational capability. Not EU army — bilateral and multilateral 
  frameworks.
- **Evidence:** European defence fragmentation means 27 nations 
  maintain separate capabilities. Pooling reduces costs. UK has 
  Lancaster House Treaties with France. Expand model.
- **Confidence:** MODERATE. Politically sensitive post-Brexit.

### 2. Resilience Investment
- **What:** The 1.5% NATO resilience target covers cyber, critical 
  infrastructure, energy security, supply chain resilience. Treat 
  this as cross-domain investment that benefits defence AND civilian 
  domains.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Dual-use investment.

### 3. Drone and Autonomous Systems Industrial Strategy
- **What:** Ukraine proves cheap drones transform warfare. UK should 
  build a sovereign drone manufacturing capability. Cross-domain with 
  Technology/Energy.
- **Evidence:** Ukraine's drone warfare costs 1/100th of 
  traditional platforms per kill. UK has aerospace expertise to 
  lead in this space.
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Evidence from Ukraine is unambiguous.

### 4. Military Housing Reform
- **What:** Transfer service family housing from MOD to a dedicated 
  housing body. Current accommodation is a scandal and a major 
  driver of recruitment/retention crisis.
- **Evidence:** NAO reports on military housing conditions. Direct 
  link to personnel shortfall.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.
